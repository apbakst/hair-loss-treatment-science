# Finasteride Evidence Breakdown

**Treatment:** Finasteride (Propecia 1 mg / Proscar 5 mg)
**Category:** FDA-Approved
**Evidence Grade:** A
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-18

---

## Overview

Finasteride is the most studied oral treatment for androgenetic alopecia (AGA) in existence. FDA-approved in 1997 as Propecia (1 mg), it is a competitive inhibitor of the type II isoenzyme of 5-alpha reductase, the enzyme that converts testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT) in androgen-sensitive tissues including hair follicles. At the 1 mg dose, finasteride reduces serum DHT by approximately 70% and scalp DHT by approximately 64%. The 5 mg formulation (Proscar) was originally approved in 1992 for benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH).

Finasteride's evidence base is among the strongest of any hair loss treatment: multiple large, multi-center, placebo-controlled randomized trials; 5-year and 10-year extension data; meta-analyses; and decades of real-world clinical use. It is one of only two FDA-approved oral treatments for male pattern hair loss (the other being dutasteride in select markets).

---

## Claim 1: Finasteride selectively inhibits type II 5-alpha reductase and reduces scalp DHT by ~64%

**The model:** Human pharmacokinetic study. Drake et al. conducted a dose-ranging study in 249 men with AGA who underwent scalp biopsies before and after receiving 0.01, 0.05, 0.2, 1, or 5 mg finasteride daily or placebo for 42 days. Scalp skin DHT, scalp skin testosterone, and serum DHT were measured via radioimmunoassay.

**The finding:** Finasteride 1 mg/day reduced scalp skin DHT levels by 64.1% and serum DHT levels by 71.4%. Notably, doses as low as 0.2 mg/day achieved near-maximal suppression of both scalp and serum DHT (56.5% and 68.6% respectively). The 5 mg dose reduced scalp DHT by 69.4% — only marginally more than 1 mg. Scalp testosterone levels increased (as expected, since less was being converted to DHT), but this does not cause hair loss because DHT, not testosterone, is the primary androgen driving follicle miniaturization.

**How much weight to give it:** Highest. This is direct, quantitative measurement of the drug's mechanism in the target tissue of human AGA patients. It establishes that finasteride reaches the scalp, inhibits the enzyme it claims to inhibit, and reduces the androgen that drives follicle miniaturization — all measured in human scalp biopsies. The dose-response data also explains why 1 mg was chosen as the clinical dose: you get ~95% of the maximal scalp DHT reduction at one-fifth the BPH dose.

**Citation:** Drake L et al. (1999). "The effects of finasteride on scalp skin and serum androgen levels in men with androgenetic alopecia." *J Am Acad Dermatol*, 41(4):550-554. PMID: 10495374.

## Claim 2: The pivotal Phase III trial (n=1,553) showed statistically significant hair regrowth at vertex over 2 years

**The model:** Two randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled Phase III trials. 1,553 men aged 18-41 with male pattern hair loss were randomized to finasteride 1 mg/day or placebo. 1,215 continued in blinded extensions for a second year. Primary endpoint was hair count in a 1-inch diameter circle (5.1 cm2) of balding vertex scalp. Secondary endpoints included investigator assessment, patient self-assessment, and standardized global photography.

**The finding:** At baseline, mean hair count in the target area was 876 hairs. Finasteride-treated men gained hair while placebo-treated men continued to lose it, resulting in a treatment difference of 107 hairs at 1 year and 138 hairs at 2 years (p<0.001 for both). By investigator assessment, 48% of finasteride patients showed increased hair growth at 1 year versus 7% on placebo. By patient self-assessment, finasteride slowed hair loss, increased hair growth, and improved appearance of hair at all timepoints.

**How much weight to give it:** Highest. This is the gold standard of hair loss evidence. Two independent trials with identical designs, 1,553 patients, 2 years of blinded follow-up, objective hair counts, and consistent results across every endpoint. This is the study that earned finasteride its FDA approval. The effect size is clinically meaningful — a 138-hair increase over a 5.1 cm2 area represents a roughly 16% increase from baseline, visible to both patients and investigators. The placebo arm's progressive decline also demonstrates what happens without treatment, making the treatment difference even more striking.

**Citation:** Kaufman KD et al. (1998). "Finasteride in the treatment of men with androgenetic alopecia." *J Am Acad Dermatol*, 39(4 Pt 1):578-589. PMID: 9777765. DOI: 10.1016/S0190-9622(98)70007-6.

## Claim 3: 5-year extension data shows durable improvement and a 93% reduction in visible hair loss progression

**The model:** Long-term multinational extension of the pivotal Phase III trials. Men from the original Kaufman 1998 trials continued in blinded, placebo-controlled extensions for up to 5 years total. Efficacy was measured by hair counts, investigator and patient assessments, and panel review of standardized clinical photographs.

**The finding:** Finasteride led to durable improvements over 5 years (p<0.001 vs placebo at all timepoints), while the placebo group showed progressive hair loss. At 5 years, finasteride treatment led to a 93% decrease relative to placebo in the likelihood of developing further visible hair loss (95% CI: 89-97%; p<0.001). Hair counts in finasteride patients peaked at approximately 2 years and were maintained above baseline through year 5, while placebo patients fell progressively below baseline.

**How much weight to give it:** Highest. Five-year blinded data from the same pivotal cohort is exceptional — very few hair loss treatments have this duration of controlled follow-up. The finding that finasteride maintains gains over 5 years while placebo deteriorates is critical for a chronic condition like AGA. The 93% reduction in visible progression is a powerful clinical result. One caveat: the 5-year completer population may be enriched for responders (patients who weren't responding may have dropped out), but this is standard for extension studies and the magnitude of the effect makes this unlikely to fully explain the results.

**Citation:** Kaufman KD et al. (2002). "Long-term (5-year) multinational experience with finasteride 1 mg in the treatment of men with androgenetic alopecia." *Eur J Dermatol*, 12(1):38-49. PMID: 11809594.

## Claim 4: Finasteride is effective for frontal/anterior scalp hair loss, though less so than vertex

**The model:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial specifically designed to assess frontal/anterior scalp response. Men with androgenetic alopecia involving the frontal/anterior mid-scalp region received finasteride 1 mg/day or placebo for 2 years. Hair counts were measured in a defined frontal scalp area.

**The finding:** Finasteride produced significant increases in frontal hair count compared to placebo (p<0.05), along with significant improvements in patient, investigator, and global photographic assessments. However, the magnitude of response was less than the vertex: in long-term studies, 89.7% of patients showed improvement at the vertex versus 61.2% in the frontal area and 44.4% at the hairline over 5 years of treatment.

**How much weight to give it:** High. This addresses a common patient concern — "will it help my hairline?" The answer is yes, but with a reality check: finasteride works best at the vertex (crown), moderately well in the frontal/mid-scalp, and least effectively at the temples/hairline. This gradient likely reflects differences in androgen receptor density and follicle biology across scalp regions. Patients expecting complete hairline restoration from finasteride alone will likely be disappointed, but slowing frontal recession and improving mid-scalp density are realistic outcomes.

**Citation:** Leyden J et al. (1999). "Finasteride in the treatment of men with frontal male pattern hair loss." *J Am Acad Dermatol*, 40(6 Pt 1):930-937. PMID: 10365924.

## Claim 5: Global photographic assessment confirms efficacy across all scalp regions in men aged 18-60

**The model:** Multicenter, double-blind, placebo-controlled studies pooling data from multiple trials. Men aged 18-41 and 41-60 with vertex hair loss were randomized to finasteride 1 mg/day or placebo for up to 24 months. Standardized global photography with expert panel assessment was used — a more clinically meaningful endpoint than hair counts alone.

**The finding:** At 24 months, finasteride produced statistically significant hair growth versus placebo in all scalp regions (p<0.05). Among younger men (18-41), 66% showed improved vertex hair growth versus 7% on placebo. Among older men (41-60), 39% improved versus 4% on placebo. Finasteride was effective in both age groups, though the response rate was higher in younger patients.

**How much weight to give it:** Highest. Global photography assessed by blinded expert panels is the clinical endpoint that matters most — it answers "can you actually see the difference?" The answer is clearly yes for most patients. The age-stratified data is clinically useful: younger patients respond better, supporting the principle of starting treatment early. Even in men over 40, nearly 4 in 10 showed visible improvement — a meaningful response rate for a daily pill.

**Citation:** Olsen EA et al. (2012). "Global photographic assessment of men aged 18 to 60 years with male pattern hair loss receiving finasteride 1 mg or placebo." *J Am Acad Dermatol*, 66(1):113-120. PMID: 22325459. DOI: 10.1016/j.jaad.2011.10.027.

## Claim 6: 10-year data from Japan shows sustained efficacy with ~91.5% of patients maintaining improvement

**The model:** Long-term open-label observational study. 523 Japanese men with AGA received finasteride 1 mg/day for 10 years. Efficacy was assessed using the Norwood-Hamilton scale and modified global photographic assessment (standardized 7-point rating score) with scalp photographs.

**The finding:** At 10 years, 91.5% of patients showed improvement (score of 5 or higher on the 7-point scale) and 99.1% showed at least prevention of disease progression (score of 4 or higher). Mean Norwood-Hamilton classification improved by approximately 1 grade, from 3.35 at baseline to 2.55 after 10 years. No serious adverse reactions were observed during the study period.

**How much weight to give it:** Moderate-High. The 10-year duration is extraordinary and provides reassurance about long-term durability that controlled trials cannot offer due to ethical constraints on keeping patients on placebo for a decade. The caveats are real: this was open-label (no placebo control), so the natural bias toward reporting favorable outcomes applies, and 10-year completers are inherently enriched for responders. Still, the finding that 99% of men maintained at least their baseline after a decade of a progressive disease is meaningful and aligns with the controlled 5-year data.

**Citation:** Yanagisawa M et al. (2019). "Long-term (10-year) efficacy of finasteride in 523 Japanese men with androgenetic alopecia." *Clin Res Trials*, 5:273. DOI: 10.15761/CRT.1000273.

## Claim 7: Sexual side effects occur in approximately 2-4% of patients above placebo rates, and a significant nocebo effect exists

**The model:** Two complementary evidence sources. (1) Phase III pivotal trial safety data from Kaufman 1998 (n=1,553): prospective, blinded adverse event collection. (2) Mondaini 2007 nocebo study: 120 men with BPH randomized to finasteride 5 mg with or without counseling about sexual side effects — a direct test of whether knowing about side effects increases their reported incidence.

**The finding:** In the pivotal trials, drug-related sexual adverse events (decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, ejaculatory disorder) occurred in 3.8% of finasteride patients versus 2.1% on placebo — a treatment-attributable excess of approximately 1.7%. In the Mondaini nocebo study, the results were dramatic: men told about potential sexual side effects reported erectile dysfunction at 30.9% versus 9.6% in men not informed (p=0.03). Similar patterns for decreased libido (23.6% vs 7.7%) and ejaculation disorders (16.3% vs 5.7%).

**How much weight to give it:** High. The Phase III safety data from large, blinded trials is the most reliable estimate of true incidence — approximately 2-4% above placebo for sexual side effects. The Mondaini study convincingly demonstrates that the nocebo effect substantially amplifies reported side effects when patients are aware of the possibility. This does not mean sexual side effects are imaginary — they are real and occur above placebo rates — but it does mean that the rates reported in uncontrolled settings, online forums, and post-marketing surveillance are likely inflated by expectation and anxiety. The clinical implication is nuanced: patients deserve informed consent about real risks, but catastrophizing the side effect profile may itself increase adverse outcomes.

**Citation:** Kaufman KD et al. (1998). *J Am Acad Dermatol*, 39(4 Pt 1):578-589. PMID: 9777765. | Mondaini N et al. (2007). "Finasteride 5 mg and sexual side effects: how many of these are related to a nocebo phenomenon?" *J Sex Med*, 4(6):1708-1712. PMID: 17655657. DOI: 10.1111/j.1743-6109.2007.00563.x.

## Claim 8: Post-Finasteride Syndrome (PFS) — persistent sexual, cognitive, or emotional symptoms after discontinuation — remains unresolved

**The model:** Heterogeneous evidence including retrospective cohort studies, case series, patient self-report registries, and regulatory review. Key studies include: (1) A 2017 retrospective cohort involving 4,284 men reporting a 0.8% rate of persistent sexual symptoms. (2) A study finding that men taking finasteride for 206+ days had >1% incidence of persistent erectile dysfunction lasting an average of 4.2 years after discontinuation. (3) FDA review (2022) concluding that available evidence "does not provide reasonable evidence" of a causal link between finasteride and persistent sexual, depressive, or suicidal symptoms.

**The finding:** The evidence is genuinely contradictory. On one hand, multiple double-blind, placebo-controlled trials show no statistically significant excess of persistent sexual symptoms after discontinuation. On the other, retrospective studies, case series, and patient reports describe persistent symptoms in a small subset of men. The FDA has reviewed the evidence twice and declined to establish a causal link, though it has required label updates acknowledging reports. A confirmed biological mechanism for PFS has not been established, though neurosteroid depletion has been proposed as a hypothesis.

**How much weight to give it:** Moderate. This claim is treated separately from the standard side effect profile because it represents a fundamentally different question: not "does finasteride cause sexual side effects while taking it?" (yes, it does, at low rates), but "can finasteride cause permanent harm that persists after stopping?" The honest answer is: the controlled trial data does not support PFS as a common or well-characterized phenomenon, but the controlled trials were not designed to detect rare, persistent effects in susceptible individuals. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. A small number of patients do report genuine, persistent symptoms. Whether these represent a pharmacological effect, a psychological/nocebo response, or an underlying condition unmasked by finasteride use remains unresolved. Patients should be informed of the debate and make their own risk assessment.

**Citation:** Irwig MS (2012). "Persistent sexual side effects of finasteride: could they be permanent?" *J Sex Med*, 9(11):2927-2932. PMID: 22789024. | FDA (2022). Review of Citizen Petition on finasteride persistent effects.

## Claim 9: Combination of finasteride and minoxidil is superior to either treatment alone

**The model:** Multiple study designs converging on the same conclusion. (1) Hu et al. 2015: Randomized comparative trial of 450 Chinese men receiving finasteride alone, minoxidil 5% alone, or the combination for 12 months. (2) Zhu et al. 2020: Systematic review and meta-analysis of 5 RCTs evaluating combined finasteride plus topical minoxidil versus monotherapy.

**The finding:** The Zhu meta-analysis found that the combination group had significantly higher global photographic evaluation scores (p<0.00001) and more patients with marked improvement (p<0.001) compared to either monotherapy. The combination produced an increase in hair density of approximately 29.7 hairs/cm2 after 24 weeks, exceeding monotherapy results by clinically meaningful thresholds. Safety profiles were similar between combination and monotherapy groups.

**How much weight to give it:** High. The synergy is biologically logical: finasteride blocks DHT-driven miniaturization (the hormonal assault on follicles) while minoxidil prolongs the anagen phase and increases follicular blood flow (stimulating growth from a different angle). Multiple meta-analyses now support the combination, and it has become the de facto first-line recommendation in clinical practice for moderate-to-severe AGA. The effect is additive rather than merely complementary — the combination achieves results neither drug can match alone.

**Citation:** Zhu Y et al. (2020). "The Efficacy and Safety of Finasteride Combined with Topical Minoxidil for Androgenetic Alopecia: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis." *Skin Appendage Disord*, 6(3):120-127. PMID: 32166351. | Hu R et al. (2015). "Combined treatment with oral finasteride and topical minoxidil in male androgenetic alopecia: a randomized and comparative study in Chinese patients." *Dermatol Ther*, 28(5):303-308. PMID: 26031764.

## Claim 10: Finasteride is contraindicated in women of childbearing potential due to teratogenicity

**The model:** Animal teratogenicity studies (required for FDA approval) plus limited clinical trials in women. Animal models showed that finasteride exposure during fetal development causes abnormalities of the external genitalia in male offspring. Two controlled clinical trials of finasteride 1 mg in premenopausal women with pattern hair loss showed no benefit over placebo.

**The finding:** Finasteride is a known teratogen for male fetuses — it can cause ambiguous genitalia by blocking the conversion of testosterone to DHT during critical stages of sexual differentiation (weeks 8-12 of gestation). In women of childbearing age, finasteride is absolutely contraindicated regardless of contraception status, and women should not even handle crushed or broken tablets. In post-menopausal women, finasteride at higher doses (2.5-5 mg) has shown some benefit in observational studies, though controlled trial data is limited and inconsistent. The 1 mg dose that works in men appears insufficient for most women.

**How much weight to give it:** Highest for the safety concern (teratogenicity is well-established from animal data and biological mechanism). Moderate for the clinical question of efficacy in post-menopausal women — there is enough signal to suggest it can work at higher doses in select patients, but the evidence base is far weaker than for men.

**Citation:** Stout SM, Stumpf JL (2010). "Finasteride treatment of hair loss in women." *Ann Pharmacother*, 44(6):1090-1097. PMID: 20442354. | Propecia FDA label (2012). FDA.gov.

## Evidence That's Missing

- **Head-to-head RCT of finasteride vs. dutasteride** at their approved AGA doses with standardized endpoints and long-term follow-up. Dutasteride inhibits both type I and type II 5-alpha reductase, but no definitive trial has established whether the additional inhibition translates to meaningfully better outcomes with an acceptable safety profile.
- **Prospective, adequately powered study specifically designed to detect Post-Finasteride Syndrome.** The existing controlled trials were not designed for this question, and retrospective studies cannot establish causation. A well-designed prospective cohort with pre-treatment baseline assessments of sexual, cognitive, and emotional function would begin to resolve the debate.
- **Large RCTs of topical finasteride vs. oral finasteride** with long-term follow-up. Early data suggests topical finasteride reduces scalp DHT with less systemic DHT suppression, but whether this translates to equivalent efficacy with fewer side effects over years of use remains unproven.
- **Genetic or biomarker-based prediction of finasteride response.** Not all patients respond equally, and there is no validated method to predict who will be a good responder before starting treatment.

---

## The Bottom Line

Finasteride is the most rigorously studied hair loss treatment available. Multiple large, multi-center, placebo-controlled randomized trials with up to 5 years of blinded data and 10 years of open-label follow-up consistently demonstrate that it slows hair loss, regrows hair, and improves clinical appearance in the majority of men with AGA. The 1 mg dose reduces scalp DHT by ~64%, which is sufficient to meaningfully slow or partially reverse the miniaturization process.

The side effect profile is real but manageable for most patients. Sexual side effects (decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, ejaculatory changes) occur in roughly 2-4% of men above placebo rates in controlled trials, and a significant nocebo effect inflates reported rates in real-world settings. The Post-Finasteride Syndrome debate remains genuinely unresolved — controlled trial data does not support it as a common phenomenon, but the trials were not designed to detect rare persistent effects, and a small number of patients do report genuine lasting symptoms.

Finasteride works best at the vertex, moderately well in the frontal scalp, and is most effective when started early. Combining it with minoxidil produces additive benefits supported by meta-analytic evidence. It is contraindicated in women of childbearing potential due to teratogenicity. For men with AGA, finasteride — alone or in combination with minoxidil — remains the evidence-based first-line treatment.
