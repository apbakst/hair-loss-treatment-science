# PP405 for Hair Loss: What the Science Actually Shows (and What's Still Missing)

*A claim-by-claim breakdown of the evidence behind PP405 (JXL-069), a first-in-class mitochondrial pyruvate carrier inhibitor being developed by Pelage Pharmaceuticals for androgenetic alopecia.*

---

PP405 is one of the most talked-about investigational hair loss treatments in 2025-2026, and for good reason: it operates through a genuinely novel mechanism (metabolic reprogramming of hair follicle stem cells), it's backed by foundational science published in Nature Cell Biology, and its Phase 2a trial results suggest it may be able to regrow terminal hair from dormant follicles -- something existing treatments can't reliably do.

But PP405 is also pre-approval, early-stage, and supported almost entirely by company-reported data. Here's every major claim, matched to the actual evidence behind it, so you can assess the hype-to-substance ratio for yourself.

---

## How to Read This Breakdown

Each claim is evaluated based on the experimental model that generated the evidence. The hierarchy matters:

| Evidence Level | What It Means | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| **In vivo animal** | Experiments in living mice. Whole-organism data, but mouse hair biology differs from human. | Moderate |
| **Ex vivo** (human tissue) | Real human hair follicles maintained alive outside the body. Architecture intact, but no blood supply or hormonal context. | Moderate-High |
| **Phase 1 human trial** | First-in-human safety/target engagement study. Not designed to prove efficacy. | Moderate |
| **Phase 2a RCT** | Randomized, placebo-controlled trial in patients. Tests for early efficacy signal. | Moderate-High |

## Claim 1: "Lactate dehydrogenase activity drives hair follicle stem cell activation"

**The model:** In vivo transgenic mouse models and in vitro mouse hair follicle stem cell cultures. Researchers generated conditional knockout mice lacking Ldha (lactate dehydrogenase A) specifically in hair follicle stem cells, and separately generated conditional knockout mice lacking Mpc1 (mitochondrial pyruvate carrier 1) to force increased lactate production. Hair cycling was tracked over time.

**The finding:** Deletion of Ldha in hair follicle stem cells prevented their activation -- follicles remained stuck in telogen (the resting phase). Conversely, deletion of Mpc1 (which blocks pyruvate from entering mitochondria, forcing lactate production via LDH) accelerated HFSC activation and the hair cycle. The researchers also identified small molecules that could topically induce the hair cycle in mice by increasing lactate production.

**How much weight to give it:** **High for establishing the biological rationale.** This was published in Nature Cell Biology (2017), one of the top journals in cell biology, and uses rigorous genetic tools -- conditional knockouts and lineage tracing -- to establish a causal (not merely correlational) link between lactate metabolism and hair follicle stem cell activation. The logic chain is clean: block lactate production and follicles stay dormant; boost lactate production and follicles activate.

Two important caveats. First, this was done entirely in mice, which have a synchronized hair cycle fundamentally different from humans. Mouse results in hair biology frequently fail to translate -- this is one of the best-known pitfalls in the field. Second, the principal investigators (William Lowry, Heather Christofk) went on to co-found Pelage Pharmaceuticals, so they have a direct financial interest in these findings being translatable. The science is rigorous, but it has not been independently replicated by groups without a commercial stake.

**Citation:** Flores A et al., "Lactate dehydrogenase activity drives hair follicle stem cell activation." Nature Cell Biology. 2017;19(9):1017-1026. PMID: 28812580.

## Claim 2: "Novel MPC inhibitors were developed with improved potency and induced hair growth in mice"

**The model:** Medicinal chemistry optimization of UK-5099 analogues. Researchers synthesized novel mitochondrial pyruvate carrier inhibitor analogues, tested their ability to increase cellular lactate production in vitro, and applied lead compounds topically to telogen-phase C3H mice to assess hair cycle induction.

**The finding:** Analogues with a 3,5-bis(trifluoromethyl)benzyl group at the N1 position showed substantially better activity than the parent compound UK-5099 at increasing cellular lactate production. When applied topically to mice, lead compounds induced the transition from telogen to anagen, visible as hair regrowth in treated areas.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate.** This is a peer-reviewed paper in the Journal of Medicinal Chemistry (2021), a respected journal, and it bridges the gap between basic discovery and a drugable compound. The fact that topical application worked in mice means the compounds can penetrate skin and reach follicles -- an important practical hurdle cleared.

But the C3H mouse model is among the most permissive hair growth models in preclinical research. Many compounds that induce anagen in C3H mice fail in humans. This paper reports on the broader class of optimized MPC inhibitors from which PP405 (JXL-069) was eventually selected, not on PP405 itself.

**Citation:** Liu X et al., "Development of Novel Mitochondrial Pyruvate Carrier Inhibitors to Treat Hair Loss." J Med Chem. 2021;64(4):2046-2063. PMID: 33534563.

## Claim 3: "PP405 activates human hair follicle stem cells ex vivo, increasing Ki67 and LDH activity within 24 hours"

**The model:** Ex vivo human facelift skin. Researchers applied single topical applications of PP405 at 0.006% and 0.06% concentrations to excised human skin containing intact hair follicles. Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) activity and Ki67 (a marker of cell proliferation) were measured in hair follicle stem cells at 24 hours.

**The finding:** Both concentrations of PP405 increased LDH activity in hair follicle stem cells within 24 hours. This corresponded with a significant increase in Ki67 signal in the hair bulge, indicating that quiescent stem cells had entered a proliferative state. The findings demonstrated proof of mechanism in human tissue consistent with the mouse models.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate-High.** This is a critical translational data point. The biggest question after Claims 1 and 2 was whether the lactate/LDH mechanism discovered in mice operates in human follicles. This ex vivo data suggests it does -- PP405 increased both LDH activity and Ki67 in real human skin.

Ex vivo human skin is valuable because tissue architecture is intact. It's more relevant than cell culture and avoids species-translation issues. But this data was presented at the AAD 2024 annual meeting, not published in a peer-reviewed journal. Conference presentations undergo less scrutiny than journal publications. No independent group has replicated these findings. And ex vivo skin lacks blood flow, immune context, and hormonal environment -- a 24-hour proliferative signal in excised skin does not guarantee sustained hair regrowth in a living person.

**Citation:** Pelage Pharmaceuticals, "Inhibition of pyruvate oxidation activates human hair follicle stem cells ex vivo." Late-breaking oral presentation, AAD 2024 Annual Meeting, San Diego.

## Claim 4: "Phase 1 trial showed PP405 was safe and produced statistically significant Ki67 activation in patient scalps after 7 days"

**The model:** Phase 1 clinical trial in patients with androgenetic alopecia. Participants applied 0.05% PP405 topical gel to the scalp daily for 7 days. Primary endpoints were safety, tolerability, and pharmacokinetics. Ki67 immunostaining of scalp biopsies served as a pharmacodynamic biomarker. The trial concluded in January 2024.

**The finding:** PP405 was safe and well-tolerated. The target pharmacokinetic profile was achieved (minimal/no systemic absorption). There was a statistically significant increase in Ki67 signal in hair follicle stem cells compared to baseline after 7 days of treatment.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate.** Demonstrating target engagement in living patients is a critical drug development milestone -- many compounds that work in mice and ex vivo tissue fail here. That 7 days of topical PP405 produced significant Ki67 increases in scalp biopsies means the drug is reaching follicles and triggering stem cell proliferation in humans.

But Ki67 positivity does not equal hair regrowth. Many interventions can induce transient proliferation without producing clinically meaningful outcomes. Phase 1 trials are designed for safety, not efficacy -- the sample sizes are tiny, treatment duration is minimal, and the actual numbers (exact patient count, Ki67 fold-change, confidence intervals) have not been made publicly available in detail. This data has not been published in a peer-reviewed journal.

**Citation:** Pelage Pharmaceuticals, Phase 1 clinical data presented at AAD 2024 Annual Meeting, San Diego.

## Claim 5: "Phase 2a: 31% of men with advanced hair loss showed >20% hair density increase vs. 0% on placebo"

**The model:** Phase 2a randomized, double-blind, vehicle-controlled trial (NCT06393452). 78 men and women with androgenetic alopecia (men: Norwood-Hamilton III vertex to V; women: Savin I-2 to I-4) were randomized to PP405 0.05% gel or vehicle placebo, applied once daily for 4 weeks, then followed through Week 12. Primary endpoint was safety/PK. Efficacy measures were secondary/exploratory.

**The finding:** PP405 was well tolerated with no systemic absorption detected. At Week 8 (4 weeks after treatment ended), 31% of men with a higher degree of hair loss treated with PP405 exhibited a >20% increase in hair density, compared to 0% in the placebo group. PP405 reportedly induced new terminal hair growth from follicles where no hair was previously present.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate -- promising but with significant limitations.** The headline numbers are genuinely interesting: 31% vs. 0% is a clean separation from placebo. The claim of new terminal hair from previously dormant follicles, if verified, would be remarkable -- existing treatments primarily thicken miniaturized hairs rather than regenerate from fully dormant follicles.

But the details temper enthusiasm:

**Subgroup analysis.** The 31% figure applies to men with "a higher degree of hair loss," not the entire treatment arm. The overall response rate has not been disclosed. Subgroup analyses in small trials are prone to cherry-picking.

**Small sample size.** N=78 across both sexes, split between PP405 and placebo, means roughly 39 per arm at most. The male subgroup with advanced loss is even smaller -- possibly 15-20 patients. A few lucky responders can swing percentages dramatically at this scale.

**Only 4 weeks of treatment.** Standard AGA trials run 6-12 months because hair cycling is slow. Four weeks is exceptionally short. The follow-up to Week 12 provides some durability signal, but the total observation window is minimal by hair loss trial standards.

**No mean hair count data.** Only a responder threshold (>20% increase) has been reported. This makes direct comparison with minoxidil or finasteride trials -- which report mean hairs/cm2 changes -- impossible.

**No disclosed p-values for efficacy.** The primary endpoint was safety, not efficacy. The efficacy findings may be exploratory and not powered for statistical significance.

**Company press release only.** No peer-reviewed publication, no independent data audit, no patient-level data shared.

**Citation:** Pelage Pharmaceuticals press release, June 2025 (BusinessWire); ClinicalTrials.gov NCT06393452.

## Claim 6: "An independent study found MPC inhibition activates the integrated stress response and causes proliferative arrest in human hair follicles"

**The model:** Ex vivo human hair follicle organ culture. Researchers at the University of Manchester treated human scalp hair follicles with UK-5099 (a well-characterized MPC inhibitor and the parent compound class of PP405). They measured proliferation, performed transcriptomic analysis, and used RNAScope to visualize gene expression changes in intact follicle sections.

**The finding:** MPC inhibition with UK-5099 induced metabolic stress-responsive proliferative arrest throughout the human hair follicle epithelium, including in Keratin 15+ hair follicle stem cells. Transcriptomics revealed disrupted FGF, IGF, TGF-beta, and Wnt signaling, mitochondrial dysfunction, and activation of the integrated stress response (ISR) via ATF4. The ISR inhibitor ISRIB attenuated both the gene expression changes and the proliferative block.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate-High as a counterpoint.** This is critically important because it comes from an independent research group with no ties to Pelage Pharmaceuticals, was published in a peer-reviewed journal (PLOS ONE, 2024), and raises genuine mechanistic concerns about the MPC inhibition approach.

The apparent contradiction with Pelage's data (which shows proliferation increases) needs resolution. Several explanations are possible:

1. **Different compounds.** UK-5099 and PP405 may differ in potency, selectivity, or pharmacokinetics enough to produce different biological outcomes.
2. **Dose matters.** The concentration and duration of MPC inhibition in ex vivo organ culture versus in vivo topical application may produce fundamentally different responses.
3. **Hormesis.** There may be a biphasic response where mild metabolic stress activates stem cells while strong stress causes arrest.
4. **Timepoint differences.** Pelage's Ki67 signal at 7-24 hours and the Manchester group's arrest signature could represent different phases of the same response -- an initial proliferative burst followed by stress-mediated shutdown.

Regardless of the explanation, this paper means the mechanism behind PP405 is not as simple as "block pyruvate transport, get more lactate, grow more hair." Long-term safety and efficacy data from Phase 3 trials will be essential to determine whether chronic MPC inhibition helps or harms hair follicles in living patients.

**Citation:** Pye D et al., "Activation of the integrated stress response in human hair follicles." PLOS ONE. 2024;19(6):e0303742. PMID: 38900734.

## The Evidence That's Missing

After matching every claim to its model, the picture is clear: PP405 has a stronger scientific foundation than most investigational hair loss treatments, but it also has critical gaps:

- **No peer-reviewed publication of any PP405-specific clinical data.** All Phase 1 and Phase 2a results have been disclosed only through company press releases and conference presentations.
- **No mean hair count change (hairs/cm2) from the Phase 2a trial.** Only a responder threshold in a subgroup has been reported, making comparison with existing treatments impossible.
- **No long-term treatment data beyond 4 weeks.** AGA is chronic and requires sustained treatment. Whether a 4-week metabolic intervention produces durable hair growth over 6-12 months is entirely unknown.
- **No resolution of the mechanistic tension** between Pelage's data (MPC inhibition activates HFSCs) and the Pye et al. 2024 finding (MPC inhibition triggers stress-mediated proliferative arrest). Which outcome dominates with chronic use?
- **No independent replication** of any PP405-specific finding by researchers without financial ties to Pelage.
- **No head-to-head or combination data** with existing approved treatments (minoxidil, finasteride).

## The Bottom Line

PP405 represents one of the most scientifically grounded new approaches to hair loss treatment in years. Unlike most investigational hair loss compounds, its foundational science was published in a top-tier journal (Nature Cell Biology, 2017) and establishes a clear causal link between a specific metabolic pathway and the activation of hair growth. The drug development path -- from basic discovery through medicinal chemistry, ex vivo proof of mechanism, Phase 1 target engagement, and Phase 2a efficacy signal -- is rigorous translational science.

The Phase 2a results are promising: 31% of men with advanced hair loss showed meaningful density increases versus 0% on placebo after only 4 weeks of treatment, with claims of new terminal hair from dormant follicles. If these findings hold up in larger, longer trials, PP405 would represent a genuinely novel mechanism in a field dominated by two 30-year-old drugs.

But the evidence has real gaps. No PP405-specific data has been peer-reviewed. The Phase 2a trial was small, the treatment period was extremely short, key metrics have not been disclosed, and an independent study raises important mechanistic questions about whether chronic MPC inhibition helps or harms follicles. Phase 3 trials planned for 2026, backed by $120 million in Series B funding from ARCH Venture Partners and GV (Google Ventures), should begin to provide answers.

**The strongest honest claim you can make about PP405 right now:** It has the best-supported novel mechanism of any investigational hair loss drug, but it is still very early -- the clinical data is limited to one small, short trial with no peer-reviewed publication, and independent research raises mechanistic questions that remain unresolved. Watch Phase 3 closely.
