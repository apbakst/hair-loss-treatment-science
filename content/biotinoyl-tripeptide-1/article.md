# Biotinoyl Tripeptide-1 (Procapil): What the Science Actually Shows

*A claim-by-claim breakdown of the evidence behind biotinoyl tripeptide-1 and Procapil -- separating the peptide from the plant actives, and what the numbers actually mean.*

---

Biotinoyl tripeptide-1 is a synthetic molecule that conjugates biotin (vitamin B7) to the tripeptide GHK (glycyl-histidyl-lysine). You'll encounter it most often as a component of Procapil, a proprietary anti-hair-loss complex developed by Sederma (now part of Croda International Group).

Like Capixyl, there's a critical distinction that product marketing rarely makes clear: Procapil is not just a peptide. It's a three-ingredient complex, and the anti-DHT activity that drives much of its theoretical appeal comes from a plant-derived triterpene, not from the peptide.

---

## Critical Distinction: What Is Procapil?

**Procapil = Biotinoyl Tripeptide-1 + Apigenin + Oleanolic Acid**

- **Biotinoyl Tripeptide-1 (Biotin-GHK):** A matrikine peptide that stimulates ECM proteins (laminin, collagen IV, vimentin) for hair anchoring and delivers biotin to follicular cells.
- **Apigenin:** A citrus-derived flavonoid with vasodilatory effects for scalp microcirculation.
- **Oleanolic Acid:** An olive leaf triterpene that inhibits 5-alpha reductase / DHT production.

Developed by Sederma (Croda International), Le Perray-en-Yvelines, France. Most clinical studies test the full Procapil combination or multi-ingredient formulations -- not biotinoyl tripeptide-1 alone.

---

## How to Read Scientific Evidence: A Quick Hierarchy

Before we get into the claims, you need to understand the evidence ladder. Not all studies are created equal:

| Evidence Level | What It Is | Weight for a Consumer | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **In silico** (computational) | Computer analysis of gene expression databases | Very Low | Predicts what *might* happen based on patterns. No biological system was actually tested. |
| **In vitro** (cell culture) | Cells grown in a dish, treated with a compound | Low | Shows biological activity in isolated cells, but cells in a plastic dish don't behave like cells in your scalp. |
| **In vivo animal** | Living mice, rats, etc. | Moderate | Whole-organism data, which matters. But animal models have limitations for human hair loss. |
| **Ex vivo** (organ culture) | Real human tissue maintained alive outside the body | Moderate-High | Tissue architecture is intact, but no blood flow, hormones, or immune context. |
| **Human clinical trial (open-label)** | People use the treatment and results are measured, but no placebo group | High | Real human data. But without placebo control, you cannot separate the treatment effect from natural variation. |
| **Human RCT (double-blind, placebo-controlled)** | Gold standard. Patients get either the real treatment or a dummy, and neither they nor the doctors know which. | Highest | The only design that definitively proves a treatment works. |

Keep this ladder in mind as we go through each claim.

## Part 1: Hair Claims

## Claim 1: "Biotinoyl tripeptide-1 produces a 58% reduction in hair loss"

**The model:** Ex vivo -- human hair follicle organ culture (follicles maintained alive in culture medium for 14 days). This experiment was conducted by BIOALTERNATIVES (a contract research organization) on behalf of Sederma. It tested the peptide alone (not the full Procapil complex).

**The finding:** At 2 ppm biotinyl-GHK (equivalent to 1% Procapil), hair follicles showed +58% superior hair growth compared to untreated controls at day 14. At 5 ppm, the same +58% increase. For comparison, minoxidil at 2 ppm (10 micromol/L) achieved +121% growth vs control in the same assay.

**How much weight to give it:** **Low-Moderate.** Here's the critical detail: the "58%" refers to hair follicle elongation in an organ culture dish over 14 days. It has been widely repackaged in marketing as a "58% reduction in hair loss" -- which sounds like a clinical trial result showing 58% fewer hairs falling out. It's not. It's ex vivo data showing follicles in a lab dish grew 58% longer with the peptide than without it. That's a meaningful biological signal, but (a) it's never been published in a peer-reviewed journal (only in the Sederma technical file), (b) organ culture doesn't replicate the scalp environment (no blood flow, hormones, or immune system), and (c) minoxidil outperformed it 2:1 in the same assay.

**Citation:** Sederma Technical File, 2012 (proprietary, not peer-reviewed). Conducted by BIOALTERNATIVES.

---

## Claim 2: "Biotinoyl tripeptide-1 produces a 35% increase in hair density"

**The model:** Unverifiable. After extensive searching, no study was found that reports a 35% increase in measured hair density for biotinoyl tripeptide-1 or Procapil.

**The finding:** The number "35.7%" does appear in one clinical study (Garre et al., 2018), but it refers to the percentage of participants (35.7% of 56 patients) who were rated as having "thicker, more voluminous hair" by clinical assessment -- NOT a 35% increase in hair density. This appears to be the origin of the misquoted claim: "35.7% of people rated as improved" was transformed into "35% increase in density" somewhere in the marketing pipeline.

**How much weight to give it:** **Very Low.** Same pattern we saw with Capixyl's claims: a real number gets pulled out of context and transformed into a different, more impressive-sounding metric. 35.7% of patients showing subjective improvement is a very different claim than 35% measured density increase. Be wary of any product citing this number.

**Citation:** Garre et al., 2018 (multi-ingredient study, open-label, no control group) -- the 35.7% figure is a response rate, not a density measurement.

---

## Claim 3: "Biotinoyl tripeptide-1 strengthens hair anchoring through structural proteins"

**The model:** Ex vivo -- human hair follicle organ culture, 14 days, treated with biotinyl-GHK. Measured via fluorescent antibody staining and DNA array analysis. Conducted by Sederma (proprietary data).

**The finding:** Peptide-treated follicles maintained laminin-5 and collagen IV layer thickness, while untreated follicles lost most of their structural proteins over the culture period. DNA array analysis showed adhesion complex proteins (vimentin, desmoglein, desmocollin, fibronectin receptor, laminin-binding protein) and cell proliferation markers were upregulated by 30-90% over baseline.

**How much weight to give it:** **Low-Moderate.** The protein data is biologically interesting -- these are the right proteins for hair anchoring, and the upregulation is meaningful in magnitude. But: (1) this is manufacturer data that has never been peer-reviewed, (2) it's ex vivo organ culture, not human scalp in vivo, (3) no direct measure of anchoring force (like a pull test) was performed, and (4) whether these protein changes translate to clinically meaningful hair retention on a living scalp is unknown.

**Citation:** Sederma Technical File, 2012 (proprietary). Not independently replicated.

---

## Claim 4: "Procapil inhibits DHT through 5-alpha reductase inhibition"

**The model:** In vitro and in vivo -- the DHT inhibition comes from oleanolic acid (the olive leaf extract component), NOT from biotinoyl tripeptide-1.

**The finding:** Oleanolic acid inhibited 5-alpha reductase enzyme by 68% and inactivated 54% of testosterone conversion to DHT in a 3% formulation on skin. In a 2023 mouse study, oleanolic acid reduced DHT, 5-alpha reductase, TNF-alpha, and TGF-beta1 levels in dorsal skin -- reducing DHT to a greater extent than minoxidil. It also stimulated VEGF and Wnt/beta-catenin pathway activity.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate (for oleanolic acid); None (for the peptide).** Like Capixyl's biochanin A, the anti-DHT effect is real but belongs to the plant-derived component, not the peptide. If you're buying a product "because of the peptide" and the mechanism you care about is DHT reduction, you're attributing the effect to the wrong ingredient. Oleanolic acid is widely available and doesn't require an expensive peptide delivery vehicle.

**Citation:** Sederma Technical File; mouse skin study, 2023 (PMID 37016778).

## Claim 5: "Clinical trials demonstrate Procapil's efficacy for hair loss"

**The model:** Multiple human studies, but all testing multi-ingredient formulations:

**Study A (Sederma pilot, ~2005):** N=35 (18 active), RCT, 3% Procapil lotion vs placebo, 4 months. 67% of treated volunteers showed improved A/T ratio. Never peer-reviewed.

**Study B (Fahim et al., 2024):** N=140, RCT, 12 months. 5% Procapil alternated with minoxidil vs minoxidil alone. Combination showed better hair count, satisfaction, and dermatologist assessment. Does not isolate Procapil from minoxidil synergy.

**Study C (Samadi et al., 2024):** N=20, single-arm (no control), 12 weeks. Caffeine + Procapil 3%. 84.2% rated as improved. Tested with caffeine, no control group.

**Study D (Pavithra & Rajashekar, 2023):** N=54, RCT, 6 months. Procapil+PRP vs Redensyl+PRP. Procapil+PRP showed 11.9% improvement vs Redensyl combo's 21.9% -- Procapil was the LESS effective group.

**The finding:** The manufacturer's pilot shows a signal vs placebo. The largest independent RCT (N=140) shows benefit when combined with minoxidil. The head-to-head with Redensyl combination was unfavorable for Procapil.

**How much weight to give it:** **Low-Moderate.** The clinical evidence is real but weak. Key issues: (1) The only placebo-controlled test of Procapil alone was the manufacturer's unpublished pilot with just 18 active subjects. (2) The strongest independent trial (N=140) tested Procapil WITH minoxidil, not alone. (3) The one head-to-head comparison that included a Procapil arm (Pavithra 2023) showed it performed worse than a Redensyl combination. (4) No study has ever tested biotinoyl tripeptide-1 in isolation in humans.

**Citation:** Sederma Technical File; Fahim et al., 2024 (J Pak Assoc Dermatol); Samadi et al., 2024 (J Cosmet Dermatol, PMID pending); Pavithra & Rajashekar, 2023 (Cureus, PMC10246929).

## Part 2: Biotinoyl Tripeptide-1 Beyond Hair

## Claim 6: "Biotin-GHK conjugation enhances follicular delivery and has applications in wound healing"

**The model:** In vitro and in vivo (rat dermal wound model). The wound healing evidence comes from Arul et al. (2005, 2007), who incorporated biotinylated GHK into collagenous matrices and tested them on rat dermal wounds. Also: Ferraro et al. (2023) synthesized Cu(II)-BioGHK and demonstrated antioxidant, antiglycant, and anti-amyloid aggregation properties in vitro.

**The finding:** Biotinylated GHK in collagen matrices improved wound contraction, increased cell proliferation, and showed high antioxidant enzyme expression in rat wounds. The Cu(II)-BioGHK complex showed greatest anti-aggregant activity against amyloid-beta aggregation, suggesting potential neuroprotective applications. The biotin conjugation preserves GHK's copper-binding ability while potentially enhancing cellular uptake and delivery.

**How much weight to give it:** **Low-Moderate.** The wound healing data is peer-reviewed and from independent academic labs, which gives it more credibility than the hair-specific claims. The Alzheimer's/amyloid-beta finding is early-stage but interesting. The delivery enhancement rationale (biotin enabling better cellular uptake) is biologically plausible. However, these are rat wounds and test-tube experiments -- translation to human applications needs clinical validation.

**Citation:** Arul et al., 2005 (J Biomed Mater Res, PMID 15803494); Arul et al., 2007 (PMID 17049946); Ferraro et al., 2023 (Molecules, PMC10538196).

## The Evidence That's Missing

- Any peer-reviewed clinical trial testing biotinoyl tripeptide-1 alone for hair.
- Any peer-reviewed publication of the Sederma pilot clinical trial data.
- An independent, placebo-controlled trial of Procapil alone (not combined with minoxidil, caffeine, or other actives).
- Proof that biotin conjugation actually enhances follicular delivery compared to unconjugated GHK or biotin alone.
- Verifiable sources for the "58% reduction in hair loss" and "35% increase in density" claims as commonly marketed.
- Head-to-head comparison of Procapil vs standard-of-care (5% minoxidil or finasteride) with adequate sample size.

## Biotinoyl Tripeptide-1 vs Acetyl Tetrapeptide-3: A Comparison

These two peptides are frequently used together in hair products (Procapil + Capixyl, often with Redensyl as the "RCP" combination). Here's how they compare:

| | Biotinoyl Tripeptide-1 (Procapil) | Acetyl Tetrapeptide-3 (Capixyl) |
|---|---|---|
| Peptide mechanism | ECM proteins + biotin delivery | ECM proteins (collagen III, laminin) |
| Anti-DHT component | Oleanolic acid (olive leaf) | Biochanin A (red clover) |
| Published peer-reviewed trial | No (manufacturer pilot only) | Yes (Loing 2013, N=30) |
| Independent clinical data | Only in multi-ingredient formulas | Only in multi-ingredient formulas |
| Peptide tested alone in humans | Never | Never |
| Best evidence | Ex vivo organ culture (+58% growth) | In vitro fibroblast ECM data |
| Overall strength | Weak | Slightly better (has peer-reviewed RCT) |

## The Bottom Line

Biotinoyl tripeptide-1 has a plausible mechanism (biotin-conjugated GHK for ECM stimulation and nutrient delivery) and some biological signal in ex vivo organ culture. As part of Procapil, the combination includes oleanolic acid (a genuine DHT inhibitor) and apigenin (a vasodilator), which provide additional hair-relevant mechanisms.

But the evidence is weaker than almost any other peptide in this guide. The key "58% reduction in hair loss" claim is ex vivo organ culture data that has been widely misrepresented as clinical evidence. The "35% density increase" appears to be a misquoted response rate. The only placebo-controlled human trial (N=18 active) has never been peer-reviewed. And the peptide has never been tested alone in humans.

**The strongest honest claim: Procapil is a three-ingredient complex with plausible biological rationale (ECM stimulation, anti-DHT via oleanolic acid, vasodilation via apigenin). There's enough signal to include it in a cosmetic maintenance routine, particularly if it's combined with proven treatments. But its evidence base is thinner than Capixyl's, and dramatically thinner than GHK-Cu's. The peptide component specifically has never been proven effective in a peer-reviewed human trial.**
