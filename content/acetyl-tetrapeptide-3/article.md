# Acetyl Tetrapeptide-3 (Capixyl): What the Science Actually Shows

*A claim-by-claim breakdown of the evidence behind Capixyl -- separating what the peptide does from what the red clover does, and what the studies actually tested.*

---

Acetyl tetrapeptide-3 is a synthetic peptide (sequence: Ac-Lys-Gly-His-Lys) most commonly sold as part of Capixyl, a proprietary complex developed by Lucas Meyer Cosmetics (now part of Clariant/IFF). You'll find it in dozens of "anti-hair-loss" serums, often alongside impressive-sounding statistics.

But here's the thing most product pages don't tell you: Capixyl is not just a peptide. It's a combination of acetyl tetrapeptide-3 AND red clover (Trifolium pratense) extract, standardized to biochanin A -- an isoflavone with its own independent anti-androgenic activity. When you see claims like "Capixyl reduces DHT," the anti-DHT effect comes from the red clover, not the peptide. And when you see clinical data, almost every study tested multi-ingredient formulations, not the peptide alone.

This distinction matters because you might be paying a premium for "the peptide" when the active ingredient doing the heavy lifting is a plant extract you could get much cheaper. Let's go claim by claim.

---

## Critical Distinction: What Is Capixyl?

**Capixyl = Acetyl Tetrapeptide-3 (peptide) + Red Clover Extract (biochanin A, isoflavone)**

INCI: Acetyl Tetrapeptide-3 (and) Butylene Glycol (and) Dextran (and) Trifolium Pratense (Clover) Flower Extract (and) Aqua. Developed by Lucas Meyer Cosmetics, Quebec, Canada.

Throughout this breakdown, we'll carefully distinguish which claims are about the peptide, which are about the red clover, and which are about the combination or multi-ingredient formulations.

## How to Read Scientific Evidence: A Quick Hierarchy

Before we get into the claims, you need to understand the evidence ladder. Not all studies are created equal:

| Evidence Level | What It Is | Weight for a Consumer | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **In silico** (computational) | Computer analysis of gene expression databases | Very Low | Predicts what *might* happen based on patterns. No biological system was actually tested. |
| **In vitro** (cell culture) | Cells grown in a dish, treated with a compound | Low | Shows biological activity in isolated cells, but cells in a plastic dish don't behave like cells in your scalp. |
| **In vivo animal** | Living mice, rats, macaques, etc. | Moderate | Whole-organism data, which matters. But animal skin is not human skin. Results often don't translate. |
| **Ex vivo** (organ culture) | Real human tissue (like a hair follicle) maintained alive outside the body | Moderate-High | Much more relevant because the tissue architecture is intact. But still tissue in a dish -- no blood flow, no hormones, no immune context. |
| **Human clinical trial (open-label)** | People use the product and results are measured, but no placebo group | High | Real human data, but without a placebo control, you can't separate the treatment effect from natural variation or placebo effect. |
| **Human RCT (double-blind, placebo-controlled)** | Gold standard. Patients get either the real treatment or a dummy, and neither they nor the doctors know which until the study ends. | Highest | This is the only design that definitively proves a treatment works. |

Keep this ladder in mind as we go through each claim.

## Part 1: Hair Claims

---

## Claim 1: "Capixyl produces a 17% increase in hair diameter"

**The model:** Unverifiable. This figure is frequently attributed to "Garcia A., Stuard B., & Wendt J. (2015). Clinical efficacy of a hair serum containing acetyl tetrapeptide-3. International Journal of Trichology, 7(4), 148-152." However, this paper does not exist on PubMed. The International Journal of Trichology Volume 7, Issue 4 (2015) does not contain this article. The citation appears to have been fabricated or hallucinated by marketing content writers and propagated across cosmetics websites.

**The finding:** The 17% figure likely originates from the Lucas Meyer Cosmetics proprietary technical data file, which is not publicly accessible as a peer-reviewed publication. The only peer-reviewed study from the manufacturer (Loing et al., 2013) reports anagen/telogen ratio changes, not hair diameter.

**How much weight to give it:** **Very Low.** You cannot give weight to a claim whose source study doesn't exist. This is a significant red flag for anyone evaluating Capixyl: one of its most-cited statistics traces back to a fabricated citation. The actual manufacturer data may show something about diameter changes, but it hasn't been published in a journal where it can be evaluated by independent scientists.

**Citation:** "Garcia et al., 2015" -- citation does not correspond to any identifiable peer-reviewed publication.

---

## Claim 2: "Capixyl produces a 67% boost in hair growth activity"

**The model:** Same situation as Claim 1 -- attributed to the same non-existent "Garcia et al., 2015" citation.

**The finding:** The only peer-reviewed study from the manufacturer (Loing et al., 2013, N=30) found a 46% increase in anagen/telogen ratio with the Capixyl combination -- not 67%. The 67% figure may come from a different metric in the proprietary technical file (the manufacturer's pilot trial reported that "67% of the 18 Capixyl volunteers showed significant improvement"), but this is a percentage of responders, not a percentage improvement in growth. It appears someone converted "67% of people improved" into "67% boost in hair growth activity" -- a fundamental misrepresentation.

**How much weight to give it:** **Very Low.** The published evidence shows a 46% improvement in A/T ratio (Loing et al., 2013), which is a reasonable metric. But the "67%" claim is either unverifiable or a misquotation of a different statistic entirely. This is common in cosmetic ingredient marketing: a real number gets separated from its context and turned into a different, more impressive-sounding claim.

**Citation:** Loing et al., 2013 (J Cosmet Sci, PMID 23449130); manufacturer technical file (proprietary).

---

## Claim 3: "Acetyl tetrapeptide-3 strengthens the ECM around follicles"

**The model:** In vitro -- MRC5 human fibroblasts (a lung-derived cell line, not scalp fibroblasts or dermal papilla cells). Cells were treated with 10^-7 M acetyl tetrapeptide-3 and measured via immunofluorescence. Also ex vivo -- human skin explants for collagen VII at the dermal-epidermal junction. This data was tested on the peptide alone (not the combination).

**The finding:** Type III collagen expression: +65% vs untreated control. Laminin expression: +285% vs control. Collagen VII at the dermal-epidermal junction was restored to baseline after corticoid-induced reduction. These are real findings from the manufacturer's published paper.

**How much weight to give it:** **Low to Moderate.** The ECM data is the strongest evidence for the peptide component specifically. The increases in collagen III and laminin are significant in magnitude. However: (1) MRC5 fibroblasts are lung cells, not scalp cells -- the same compound might behave differently in dermal papilla fibroblasts. (2) This is in vitro data -- cells in a dish don't replicate the conditions of your scalp. (3) The study was conducted by the manufacturer's own R&D department (Lucas Meyer Cosmetics), not by independent researchers. (4) No direct measurement of hair anchoring force was performed -- the claim that ECM strengthening means better anchoring is logical but untested.

**Citation:** Loing et al., 2013 (J Cosmet Sci, PMID 23449130) -- manufacturer-sponsored.

---

## Claim 4: "Capixyl reduces DHT / inhibits 5-alpha reductase"

**The model:** In vitro -- human genital skin fibroblasts and benign prostatic hyperplasia tissue homogenates. This is independent academic research from the University of Wales (Evans et al., 1995). CRITICAL: This evidence is for biochanin A (the red clover extract), NOT for acetyl tetrapeptide-3 (the peptide).

**The finding:** At 100 micromolar concentration, biochanin A caused >80% inhibition of 5-alpha reductase activity. In rat prostate tissue, the IC50 was 140 micromolar. The manufacturer claims a "93% reduction in DHT" in their marketing materials, but the source study for this specific number is not publicly available -- it likely comes from a proprietary in vitro assay.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate (for biochanin A); None (for the peptide).** The anti-DHT activity is real and independently confirmed -- but it belongs to the red clover extract, not the peptide. If a product page tells you "acetyl tetrapeptide-3 reduces DHT," that's misleading. The peptide has no demonstrated 5-alpha reductase inhibitory activity. It's an ECM stimulator, not an anti-androgen. Any product containing Capixyl gets its anti-DHT effect from biochanin A, which is a plant isoflavone available in many cheaper formulations.

**Citation:** Evans et al., 1995 (J Endocrinol, PMID 7490559) -- independent academic research.

---

## Claim 5: "Clinical trials show Capixyl improves hair growth comparable to minoxidil"

**The model:** Multiple human trials, but with important caveats:

**Study A (manufacturer, 2013):** N=30, RCT, 5% Capixyl lotion vs placebo, 4 months. Anagen density +13%, telogen density -29%, A/T ratio +46%. Manufacturer-sponsored (Loing et al.).

**Study B (independent, 2020):** N=32, triple-blind RCT, herbal combo containing Capixyl components + ginseng vs 3% minoxidil, 24 weeks. Hair count: herbal +8.3% vs minoxidil +8.68% (not significant). Hair Mass Index: herbal +13.8% vs minoxidil +31.48% (not significant). (Lueangarun & Panchaprateep.)

**Study C (2019):** N=106, RCT, Redensyl+Capixyl+Procapil (RCP) vs 5% minoxidil, 24 weeks. RCP group scored higher by researcher and photographic assessment. Published in a questionable journal. Tests triple combination.

**The finding:** The manufacturer study shows a real difference vs placebo. The independent study shows rough comparability to 3% minoxidil (not the standard 5%). The RCP study shows a triple-ingredient combination outperforming minoxidil, but you can't attribute that to Capixyl specifically.

**How much weight to give it:** **Low to Moderate.** The clinical evidence is real but thin. Key problems: (1) The manufacturer study (N=30) is small, short (4 months), and funded by the company that sells Capixyl. (2) The independent study compared against only 3% minoxidil (not the standard 5%) and included ginseng extract in the formula. (3) The RCP study tested three ingredients together. (4) No study has tested acetyl tetrapeptide-3 alone in humans. (5) The placebo group in the manufacturer study inexplicably worsened significantly (-33% A/T ratio), which inflates the apparent treatment effect. Bottom line: there's enough signal to say Capixyl probably does something, but the evidence is nowhere near the standard of proof for minoxidil or finasteride.

**Citation:** Loing et al., 2013 (PMID 23449130); Lueangarun & Panchaprateep, 2020 (PMID 33584955); Karaca & Akpolat, 2019 (Hilaris Publisher).

## Part 2: Acetyl Tetrapeptide-3 Beyond Hair

---

## Claim 6: "Acetyl tetrapeptide-3 has skin anti-aging benefits"

**The model:** In vitro only -- MRC5 fibroblasts showing collagen III and laminin stimulation (same data as Claim 3). No dedicated skin anti-aging clinical trials on acetyl tetrapeptide-3 were identified.

**The finding:** The collagen III and laminin increases seen in vitro theoretically translate to firmer, more structured skin. But this has not been tested clinically for facial anti-aging.

**How much weight to give it:** **Very Low.** The peptide is marketed almost exclusively for hair. Its ECM-stimulating mechanism could theoretically benefit skin, but without clinical data, this remains speculative. The parent peptide sequence (KGHK) is structurally related to GHK (which has extensive skin evidence when complexed with copper), but acetyl tetrapeptide-3 is not GHK-Cu and shouldn't inherit its evidence base.

**Citation:** Loing et al., 2013 (in vitro data only).

## The Evidence That's Missing

After matching every claim to its model, a clear pattern emerges: Capixyl has two plausible mechanisms (ECM stimulation from the peptide and anti-DHT activity from biochanin A), one small manufacturer-sponsored RCT, and widespread marketing claims that outstrip the evidence.

What it does *not* have:

- **Any clinical trial testing acetyl tetrapeptide-3 alone (without red clover) in humans for hair.** Every human study has tested either the full Capixyl combination or a multi-ingredient formula containing Capixyl.

- **Independent replication of the manufacturer's in vitro ECM data.** The collagen III and laminin findings come from a single manufacturer-sponsored paper.

- **A properly powered, independent RCT comparing Capixyl to standard-dose (5%) minoxidil with a placebo arm.** The existing independent study used only 3% minoxidil as a comparator and included ginseng extract.

- **Any data on whether biochanin A's in vitro 5-alpha reductase inhibition translates to meaningful DHT reduction when applied topically to human scalp** at the concentrations present in Capixyl serums.

- **Verifiable sources for the two most-cited claims (17% diameter increase, 67% growth boost).** The "Garcia et al. 2015" citation that underpins both claims does not correspond to any identifiable peer-reviewed publication.

- **Hair diameter or density data measured by any method other than TrichoScan** in the manufacturer's study.

---

## The Bottom Line

Capixyl is one of the more popular cosmetic hair-care ingredients, and it's not without merit: the manufacturer's RCT showed a real difference vs placebo, the ECM mechanism is plausible, and the red clover component has genuine (independent) anti-androgenic activity in vitro. It's a reasonable ingredient to include in a hair care routine.

But the marketing overstates the evidence significantly. The two most-cited statistics (17% diameter, 67% growth boost) can't be traced to verifiable sources. The "anti-DHT" activity belongs to the plant extract, not the peptide. And virtually all clinical data tests multi-ingredient formulations, making it impossible to know how much the peptide contributes.

**The strongest honest claim: Capixyl contains two components with plausible hair-support mechanisms -- an ECM-stimulating peptide and an anti-androgenic isoflavone -- and one small manufacturer-sponsored RCT showed improvement vs placebo. It's a reasonable cosmetic maintenance ingredient, but it should not be considered a substitute for proven treatments, and you should be skeptical of specific efficacy numbers that can't be independently verified.**
