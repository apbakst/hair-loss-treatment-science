# Liothyronine (T3) for Hair Loss: What the Science Actually Shows

*A claim-by-claim breakdown of the evidence behind topical thyroid hormone for hair growth, with the experimental model behind each finding and what it means for you.*

---

You may have seen liothyronine (synthetic T3, a thyroid hormone) listed as an ingredient in compounded hair loss formulations -- often alongside minoxidil, azelaic acid, and progesterone. The rationale sounds compelling: thyroid hormones regulate the hair cycle, hair follicles express thyroid hormone receptors and can even convert T4 to T3 locally, and hypothyroidism is a well-known cause of hair loss. So why not apply T3 directly to the scalp?

The science behind this idea is real and surprisingly deep. But the distance between "hair follicles respond to thyroid hormones in a dish" and "topical T3 regrows hair on a human scalp" is enormous -- and most of the evidence sits firmly on the laboratory side of that gap. Here is every major claim about liothyronine for hair, matched to the actual experiment behind it.

---

## How to Read Scientific Evidence: A Quick Hierarchy

| Evidence Level | What It Is | Weight for a Consumer | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **In silico** (computational) | Computer analysis of gene expression databases | Very Low | Predicts what *might* happen based on patterns. No biological system was actually tested. |
| **In vitro** (cell culture) | Cells grown in a dish, treated with a compound | Low | Shows biological activity in isolated cells, but cells in a dish don't behave like cells in your scalp. |
| **In vivo animal** | Living mice, rats, etc. | Moderate | Whole-organism data, which matters. But mouse skin is not human skin. |
| **Ex vivo** (organ culture) | Real human tissue maintained alive outside the body | Moderate-High | Much more relevant because tissue architecture is intact. But still no blood flow, immune system, or hormonal milieu. |
| **Human clinical trial (open-label)** | People use the product and results are measured, but no placebo group | High | Real human data. But without a placebo control, you can't separate treatment effect from natural variation. |
| **Human RCT (double-blind, placebo-controlled)** | Gold standard. Patients get either real treatment or placebo, neither they nor doctors know which. | Highest | The only design that definitively proves a treatment works. |

## Claim 1: "Hair follicles are direct, independent targets of thyroid hormones -- they express receptors, deiodinases, and even produce TRH"

**The model:** Ex vivo -- Microdissected human scalp hair follicles from female facelift patients (ages 40-69), organ-cultured and analyzed for thyroid hormone signaling components via immunohistochemistry, RT-PCR, and functional assays.

**The finding:** Human hair follicles express thyroid hormone receptors (TRalpha1 and TRbeta1, with TRbeta1 being the dominant isoform during anagen), deiodinase enzymes D2 and D3 (enabling local conversion of T4 to T3 and inactivation of T3), thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH) and its receptor (TRH-R), and thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) receptors. This means the hair follicle possesses the complete machinery of the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis in miniature -- it can both produce and respond to thyroid hormones independently of systemic circulation.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate-High.** This is well-established biology from multiple research groups (van Beek et al. 2008, Gaspar et al. 2010, Vidali et al. 2014, Slominski et al.). The finding that hair follicles are genuine thyroid hormone-responsive mini-organs is not disputed. It provides the strongest possible *rationale* for exploring thyroid hormones in hair biology. But establishing that a receptor system exists is different from proving that activating it topically regrows hair in people with androgenetic alopecia. Many receptor systems are present in the follicle; their presence alone does not make them therapeutic targets.

**Citation:** van Beek N et al., "Thyroid hormones directly alter human hair follicle functions: anagen prolongation and stimulation of both hair matrix keratinocyte proliferation and hair pigmentation." J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2008;93(11):4381-4388. PMID: 18728176.

## Claim 2: "T3 and T4 prolong anagen and stimulate hair matrix keratinocyte proliferation in human hair follicles"

**The model:** Ex vivo -- Human anagen VI scalp hair follicles microdissected from facelift skin of euthyroid females (mean age 56), organ-cultured for 6 days with T3 (1-100 nM) or T4 (1-100 nM).

**The finding:** T4 significantly prolonged the anagen (growth) phase of cultured human hair follicles, and this effect correlated with the downregulation of TGF-beta2, described as "the key anagen-inhibitory growth factor." T4 upregulated hair matrix keratinocyte proliferation. Both T3 and T4 downregulated apoptosis of hair matrix keratinocytes. Both T3 and T4 also significantly stimulated intrafollicular melanin synthesis (hair pigmentation) and modulated thyroid hormone-responsive keratins (CK6 upregulated, CK14 downregulated).

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate-High.** This is the foundational study for the field (van Beek et al. 2008, Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism). The ex vivo organ culture model preserves the three-dimensional architecture of intact hair follicles, making it substantially more relevant than cell culture. The TGF-beta2 downregulation mechanism is particularly interesting because TGF-beta2 is a known catagen inducer, and blocking it is a logical strategy for keeping follicles in growth phase. However, these follicles were cultured in isolation -- no blood supply, no DHT, no immune environment. The donors were older women undergoing facelifts, not AGA patients with miniaturizing follicles. Whether T3/T4 can overcome the DHT-driven miniaturization that defines androgenetic alopecia is an entirely different question that this study does not address.

**Citation:** van Beek N et al., J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2008;93(11):4381-4388. PMID: 18728176.

## Claim 3: "Topical T3 dramatically increases hair count and length in mice and rats"

**The model:** In vivo animal -- SKH-1 hairless mice treated topically with T3 (150 ng or 3.8 micrograms daily) in liposomes for 7 days; CD rats treated with T3 (0.5 or 12.8 micrograms daily) for 2 weeks.

**The finding:** Mice receiving 3.8 micrograms T3 had 42% more hairs per millimeter than controls, hair length was 1,180% longer, epidermal 3H-thymidine incorporation was 49% greater, and BrdU-stained proliferative cells were 80% more numerous. Rats receiving 12.8 micrograms T3 had 85% more hairs per millimeter, 48% greater dermal thickness, 26% greater epidermal thickness, and 130% greater thymidine incorporation. At the lower dose of 150 ng, some systemic absorption occurred (manifested as lower serum T4), but this was not significant at the lowest doses.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate.** These are striking numbers from whole-organism studies, and the Safer et al. 2001 paper remains widely cited. But the limitations of animal hair models are significant. Mice have a synchronized hair cycle (humans do not). Hairless mice have a fundamentally different follicular biology. And the doses that produced the most dramatic effects (3.8 micrograms in mice) also caused measurable systemic thyroid hormone changes (suppressed serum T4), raising the question of whether the hair effects were truly local or partly driven by systemic hormonal shifts. The results justify further investigation but cannot be directly translated to human AGA.

**Citation:** Safer JD et al., "Topical triiodothyronine stimulates epidermal proliferation, dermal thickening, and hair growth in mice and rats." Thyroid. 2001;11(8):717-724. PMID: 11525263.

## Claim 4: "Topically applied T3 prolongs anagen in intact human scalp skin ex vivo"

**The model:** Ex vivo -- Full-thickness human scalp skin from 5 healthy donors (ages 48-56) was organ-cultured for 6 days with T3 (1 nM and 10 nM) and T4 (1 microM and 10 microM) applied topically in two different vehicles (Vehicle A: 30% ethanol/50% propylene glycol; Vehicle B: 60% ethanol/20% propylene glycol). This used an FDA-approved ingredient vehicle.

**The finding:** Topical T3 at 1 nM in Vehicle A significantly increased the percentage of hair follicles remaining in anagen (p<0.05) across three donors. T3 also significantly upregulated FGF-7/keratinocyte growth factor (p<0.05 to p<0.001), a known hair growth promoter. Both T3 and T4 significantly increased keratin 15 expression in the bulge region (a stem cell marker; p<0.05 to p<0.01), stimulated IGF-1 production, and increased CD31+ endothelial cell numbers (suggesting pro-angiogenic effects). Intrafollicular melanin synthesis was not increased (unlike the isolated follicle studies). Notably, combining T3 + T4 unexpectedly inhibited anagen in Vehicle A.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate-High.** This 2024/2025 study (Paus et al., published in Clinical and Experimental Dermatology) is the most clinically relevant preclinical data to date because it used *topical* application on *intact human scalp skin* -- not isolated follicles, not cell culture. This is the closest model to how a patient would actually use the product. The FGF-7 upregulation and K15 stem cell marker data add mechanistic depth. However, the sample size was small (5 donors, 3 fully analyzed), the culture lasted only 6 days, and the unexpected inhibition from the T3+T4 combination raises questions about dose-response complexity. The authors themselves recommend 1 nM T3 in Vehicle A as the candidate for clinical testing, with intermittent pulse dosing to minimize systemic risk.

**Citation:** Paus R et al., "Pilot evidence that topically applied triiodothyronine can stimulate hair growth in human scalp skin." Clin Exp Dermatol. 2025;50(9):1862. PMID: 40275449. See also bioRxiv preprint 2024.06.11.598522.

## Claim 5: "TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone), the upstream peptide, also promotes hair growth in human follicles"

**The model:** Ex vivo -- Microdissected human scalp hair follicles organ-cultured with TRH.

**The finding:** TRH promoted hair shaft elongation, prolonged anagen, and antagonized catagen induction by TGF-beta2 in organ-cultured human hair follicles. TRH increased proliferation and inhibited apoptosis of hair matrix keratinocytes. These effects appeared to be mediated in part by reducing ATM/Atr-dependent phosphorylation of p53 (a cell death signaling pathway). The study also established that human hair follicles express TRH receptors and produce TRH themselves -- making the follicle both a source and target of this peptide.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate-High.** The Gaspar et al. 2010 study (FASEB Journal) is significant because it identifies a second potential therapeutic target in the thyroid hormone pathway for hair growth. TRH is a tripeptide (pyroGlu-His-ProNH2) that could theoretically be easier to formulate topically than T3 itself, and being the upstream regulator, it might activate the pathway more physiologically. However, the same caveats apply as for Claim 2: this is ex vivo organ culture without blood flow or systemic context, and the study did not test TRH in an AGA-relevant model. No human trial of topical TRH for hair loss has been conducted.

**Citation:** Gaspar E et al., "Thyrotropin releasing hormone (TRH): a new player in human hair-growth control." FASEB J. 2010;24(2):393-403. PMID: 19825978.

## Claim 6: "Thyroid hormones boost mitochondrial function and energy metabolism in hair follicles"

**The model:** Ex vivo -- Organ-cultured human scalp hair follicles treated with TRH (30 nM), TSH (10 mU/mL), T4 (100 nM), and T3 (100 pM).

**The finding:** All four hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis hormones increased gene and protein expression of mitochondrial respiratory chain components (MTCO1, a subunit of complex IV), mitochondrial transcription factor A (TFAM), and the voltage-dependent anion channel porin. All stimulated complex I/IV activity and mitochondrial biogenesis. T3 and TSH increased follicular heat production. T3, T4, and TRH stimulated ATP production in hair follicle keratinocytes. Critically, T3 and T4 *reduced* reactive oxygen species (ROS) formation and all tested hormones upregulated ROS scavengers (catalase, SOD2).

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate.** The Vidali et al. 2014 study (Journal of Investigative Dermatology) adds an important metabolic dimension. Hair follicles are among the most metabolically active tissues in the body, and follicular mitochondrial dysfunction has been increasingly linked to hair loss. The idea that T3 could serve as a mitochondrial "energy booster" for the follicle is biologically elegant. But this remains an ex vivo finding. Whether topically applied T3 reaches the follicle's mitochondria in sufficient concentrations to produce these effects in vivo is unknown. The reduction in ROS is a favorable safety signal (you would not want a treatment that increases oxidative stress in the follicle), but it does not prove therapeutic efficacy.

**Citation:** Vidali S et al., "Hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis hormones stimulate mitochondrial function and biogenesis in human hair follicles." J Invest Dermatol. 2014;134(1):33-42. PMID: 23949722.

## Claim 7: "A TRbeta-selective agonist promotes hair regrowth in mice via Wnt and Hedgehog pathways"

**The model:** In vivo animal -- 45 female C3H/HeJ mice (telogen-stage after depilation) treated topically with TDM10842, a thyroid hormone receptor beta1 (TRbeta1) agonist, at 0.05% concentration in 30% propylene glycol/70% ethanol. Two applications of 60 microliters each (at 0 and 24 hours).

**The finding:** The TDM10842-treated group showed visible skin graying (an early sign of anagen entry) by day 4 and hair shaft emergence by day 8. Transcriptomic analysis identified 857 differentially expressed genes. Ki-67 proliferation marker was significantly increased (p<0.0001). The mechanism involved upregulation of the Wnt/beta-catenin pathway (Wnt5a, Wnt5b, Fzd1/2, Ctnnb1, Lef1, Ccnd1) and the Sonic hedgehog pathway (Shh, Gli1/2, Ptch1/2). The gene Pclaf was identified as a critical hub connecting both pathways (Pearson correlation of 0.88 with Wnt, 0.81 with Hedgehog), with Cyclin D1 as the key downstream effector driving cell cycle progression.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate.** This study is mechanistically rich and identifies specific molecular pathways through which thyroid hormone receptor activation promotes hair growth. The Wnt/beta-catenin and Hedgehog pathways are well-validated as essential for follicle cycling and stem cell activation. The finding that a *selective* TRbeta1 agonist (rather than T3 itself, which activates both TRalpha and TRbeta) can trigger hair regrowth is important because TRbeta-selective compounds may have fewer systemic side effects. However, this is a mouse depilation model -- the hair was shaved to synchronize the cycle, and the compound accelerated the natural telogen-to-anagen transition. This does not model AGA, where follicles miniaturize under androgenic pressure. The compound (TDM10842) is not T3/liothyronine and has not been tested in humans for hair.

**Citation:** Zhi J et al., "Thyroid hormone receptor agonist promotes hair growth in mice." Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol. 2022;15:2719-2733. PMC10681092. Also: Zhi J et al., "Thyroid receptor beta: a promising target for developing novel anti-androgenetic alopecia drugs." Drug Discov Today. 2024;29(6):104001.

## Claim 8: "A clinical trial of topical T3 in alopecia areata patients showed it was safe but no better than placebo"

**The model:** Human RCT -- Double-blind, randomized pilot clinical trial. 10 patients with patchy alopecia areata. Topical triiodothyronine ointment was applied to one of two bilaterally symmetrical patches, with placebo applied to the other, twice daily for 12 weeks. Hair regrowth was assessed every 4 weeks. Thyroid function (T3, T4, TSH) and liver function were monitored.

**The finding:** After 12 weeks, there was no statistically significant difference between topical triiodothyronine and placebo for hair regrowth. Importantly, the formulation was safe -- no adverse events, no clinically significant changes in thyroid function tests.

**How much weight to give it:** **This is the highest evidence level (human RCT) but provides negative evidence.** The Nasiri et al. 2011 study is the only published placebo-controlled human trial of topical T3 for any form of hair loss, and it failed. Several important caveats: (1) This was alopecia areata (an autoimmune condition), not androgenetic alopecia -- the pathophysiology is entirely different, so the failure does not necessarily predict failure in AGA. (2) The sample size was very small (n=10), dramatically underpowered for detecting a modest effect. (3) The formulation, concentration, and vehicle were not optimized based on the later ex vivo research (which identified specific vehicle compositions and doses that work). (4) The authors themselves concluded that "newer thyroid hormone analogues might be more effective." Despite all these caveats, this study is a sobering reminder that promising preclinical data does not guarantee clinical success.

**Citation:** Nasiri S et al., "Hair regrowth with topical triiodothyronine ointment in patients with alopecia areata: a double-blind, randomized pilot clinical trial of efficacy." J Dermatolog Treat. 2012;23(6):443-446. PMID: 21521379.

## The Evidence That's Missing

After matching every claim to its model, a clear pattern emerges: the mechanistic foundation for thyroid hormones in hair biology is surprisingly strong (Claim 1-2, 5-6), the animal data is dramatic but limited in translatability (Claim 3, 7), the most clinically relevant ex vivo work is very recent and small-scale (Claim 4), and the only human clinical trial was negative -- in a different disease with a likely suboptimal formulation (Claim 8).

What liothyronine for hair loss does *not* have:

- **A placebo-controlled human trial of topical T3 for androgenetic alopecia.** The Nasiri trial was in alopecia areata. No published RCT has tested topical T3 in AGA patients -- the population that compounding pharmacies are actually targeting with their formulations.
- **Dose-response data in humans.** The ex vivo studies suggest 1 nM T3 is optimal, but no one has tested different concentrations on human scalps to determine the right dose.
- **Long-term safety data for topical thyroid hormone on the scalp.** While the Nasiri 2011 trial showed no thyroid function changes over 12 weeks, and the Safer 2001 animal data showed dose-dependent systemic absorption, there are no 6-month or 12-month safety studies. Chronic topical T3 at higher concentrations could theoretically suppress the HPT axis, cause localized thyrotoxic effects, or have other unintended consequences.
- **Head-to-head comparison with established treatments.** No study compares topical T3 to minoxidil, finasteride, or any approved therapy.
- **Evidence that topical T3 can overcome DHT-driven miniaturization.** All the positive data involves either normal (non-miniaturizing) follicles or follicles in non-AGA contexts. The central question -- can T3 counteract the effects of dihydrotestosterone on androgen-sensitive follicles -- has not been tested.

## What About Compounded Formulations?

Several compounding pharmacies now include liothyronine (typically at very low concentrations such as 2 mcg/mL) in multi-ingredient scalp solutions alongside minoxidil, azelaic acid, progesterone, and other agents. These formulations are prescribed by hair loss clinicians and are being used by real patients.

It is important to understand what this means and what it does not mean. The inclusion of T3 in these compounds reflects a reasonable mechanistic hypothesis, not proven clinical efficacy. Because these are multi-ingredient formulations, any benefit a patient experiences cannot be attributed to the T3 component specifically -- it could be driven entirely by the minoxidil or other active ingredients. No compounding pharmacy has published controlled data isolating the contribution of T3 to their formulation's overall effect.

The safety profile appears favorable at the very low concentrations used (2 mcg/mL), with pharmacy literature suggesting high local penetration and minimal systemic absorption. But this has not been rigorously validated in clinical pharmacokinetic studies.

## The Bottom Line

Liothyronine (T3) has one of the strongest *mechanistic rationales* of any off-label hair loss treatment. The biology is real: hair follicles are genuine thyroid hormone-responsive mini-organs with their own receptor systems, deiodinases, and even local TRH production. In lab settings, T3 prolongs anagen, reduces apoptosis, downregulates the catagen-inducing TGF-beta2, boosts mitochondrial function, and stimulates growth factor production. The animal data is dramatic, and the recent topical ex vivo human scalp work (Paus et al. 2024/2025) adds genuine clinical plausibility.

But "strongest rationale" and "proven treatment" are very different things. The only human clinical trial of topical T3 was negative (in a different disease, with a likely suboptimal formulation, at underpowered sample size). No human trial in AGA has been published. The evidence currently lives almost entirely in the ex vivo and animal space -- a long way from the clinic.

**The strongest honest claim you can make about liothyronine for hair: The mechanistic foundation is compelling and the recent topical ex vivo data provides genuine proof-of-concept, but there is currently no clinical evidence that topical T3 regrows hair in humans with androgenetic alopecia. It belongs in the "scientifically interesting, clinically unproven" category. If you are using a compounded formulation that contains T3, the T3 component is a reasonable addition based on mechanistic logic, but its specific contribution to any benefit you observe is unknown.**
