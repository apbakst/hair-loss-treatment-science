# Minoxidil for Hair Loss: What the Science Actually Shows

*A claim-by-claim breakdown of the evidence behind the world's most widely used hair growth drug, covering topical and oral formulations, with the experimental model behind each finding and what it means for you.*

---

Minoxidil is one of only two FDA-approved treatments for androgenetic alopecia (the other being finasteride). Originally developed in the 1960s as an oral antihypertensive, its hair-growing side effect was noticed in patients taking it for blood pressure — and the rest is pharmaceutical history. The FDA approved topical 2% minoxidil (Rogaine) in 1988, expanded it to women in 1991, moved it over-the-counter in 1996, and approved the 5% formulation in 1997-1998.

Unlike many of the compounds covered on this site, minoxidil has decades of large, well-designed randomized controlled trials behind it. But "proven" doesn't mean "perfectly understood" — its exact mechanism of action is still debated, a significant fraction of users don't respond, and a newer chapter is being written right now with low-dose oral minoxidil (LDOM).

Here's every major claim about minoxidil for hair, matched to the actual experiment behind it.

## How to Read Scientific Evidence: A Quick Hierarchy

| Evidence Level | What It Is | Weight for a Consumer | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **In silico** (computational) | Computer analysis of gene expression databases | Very Low | Predicts what *might* happen based on patterns. No biological system was actually tested. |
| **In vitro** (cell culture) | Cells grown in a dish, treated with a compound | Low | Shows biological activity in isolated cells, but cells in a dish don't behave like cells in your scalp. |
| **Ex vivo** (organ culture) | Real human tissue maintained alive outside the body | Moderate | Tissue architecture is intact, but no blood flow, hormones, or immune context. |
| **In vivo animal** | Living mice, rats, etc. | Moderate | Whole-organism data, but mouse skin is not human skin. |
| **Human clinical trial (open-label)** | People use the product; results measured, no placebo group | Moderate-High | Real human data, but without placebo control you can't isolate the treatment effect. |
| **Human RCT (double-blind, placebo-controlled)** | Gold standard — patients get treatment or placebo, neither they nor doctors know which | Highest | The only design that definitively proves a treatment works. |

## Claim 1: "Topical 5% minoxidil is superior to 2% for regrowing hair in men"

**The model:** Human RCT (double-blind, placebo-controlled). Olsen et al. conducted a 48-week, multicenter trial of 393 men aged 18-49 with androgenetic alopecia. Subjects were randomized to 5% topical minoxidil, 2% topical minoxidil, or placebo applied twice daily. Hair counts were measured in a defined target area on the vertex.

**The finding:** At 48 weeks, 5% minoxidil produced a mean increase of 18.6 nonvellus hairs/cm^2, compared to 12.7 hairs/cm^2 for 2% minoxidil and 3.9 hairs/cm^2 for placebo. That's a 45% greater regrowth with 5% vs 2%. Importantly, the onset of response was earlier with 5% — hair count differences were already detectable at week 8. More pruritus and local irritation were observed with 5%.

**How much weight to give it:** **Highest.** This is a large, well-designed, placebo-controlled RCT published in the *Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology* — the gold standard of evidence. The treatment effect is real and statistically significant, though modest in absolute terms (about 6 extra hairs per cm^2 favoring 5% over 2%). The clinical relevance of that difference varies by individual.

**Citation:** Olsen EA, Dunlap FE, Funicella T, et al. A randomized clinical trial of 5% topical minoxidil versus 2% topical minoxidil and placebo in the treatment of androgenetic alopecia in men. *J Am Acad Dermatol.* 2002;47(3):377-385.

## Claim 2: "Topical minoxidil works for women too — both 2% and 5%"

**The model:** Human RCT (double-blind, placebo-controlled). Lucky et al. conducted a 48-week, multicenter trial of 381 women aged 18-49 with female pattern hair loss (FPHL). Subjects were randomized to 5% topical minoxidil (n=153), 2% topical minoxidil (n=154), or placebo (n=74), applied twice daily.

**The finding:** At 48 weeks, mean nonvellus hair count changes in a 1 cm^2 target area were +24.5 (5% group), +20.7 (2% group), and +9.4 (placebo). Both active treatments significantly outperformed placebo (p<0.001). The 5% was numerically superior to 2% but the difference was smaller than in men, and both concentrations were effective. For women, the FDA initially approved only 2% — and some dermatologists still recommend 2% for women because 5% causes more facial hypertrichosis (unwanted facial hair) in women without a proportionally large efficacy advantage.

**How much weight to give it:** **Highest.** Another large, well-designed RCT confirming minoxidil efficacy in women. The absolute gains are actually *larger* in women than in men in this study, which is noteworthy. The smaller relative advantage of 5% over 2% in women is a legitimate reason to consider starting at 2%.

**Citation:** Lucky AW, Piacquadio DJ, Ditre CM, et al. A randomized, placebo-controlled trial of 5% and 2% topical minoxidil solutions in the treatment of female pattern hair loss. *J Am Acad Dermatol.* 2004;50(4):541-553.

## Claim 3: "Minoxidil 5% foam is as effective as 5% solution — with less irritation"

**The model:** Human RCT (double-blind, placebo-controlled). Olsen et al. conducted a 16-week, multicenter trial of 352 men aged 18-49 with AGA. Subjects were randomized to 5% minoxidil topical foam (MTF) or placebo foam, applied twice daily. A 52-week open-label extension followed.

**The finding:** At week 16, the 5% foam group had statistically significant increases in hair count versus placebo (p<0.0001) and significant improvement in subjective hair loss assessments (p<0.0001). The foam was well tolerated over 52 weeks. Critically, the foam formulation eliminates propylene glycol — the ingredient in the solution that causes contact dermatitis in approximately 7% of users (higher with 5% due to greater PG content).

**How much weight to give it:** **Highest.** This RCT established that the foam vehicle delivers equivalent efficacy while removing the most common cause of topical irritation. For patients who experience scalp itching, dryness, or redness with the solution, foam is the clear alternative. The 16-week primary endpoint is shorter than the 48-week Olsen 2002 trial, but the open-label extension provided long-term safety data.

**Citation:** Olsen EA, Whiting D, Bergfeld W, et al. A multicenter, randomized, placebo-controlled, double-blind clinical trial of a novel formulation of 5% minoxidil topical foam versus placebo in the treatment of androgenetic alopecia in men. *J Am Acad Dermatol.* 2007;57(5):767-774.

## Claim 4: "Minoxidil works by opening potassium channels and upregulating VEGF — it's not just a vasodilator"

**The model:** In vitro — human hair dermal papilla cells. Lachgar et al. cultured human dermal papilla cells and treated them with increasing concentrations of minoxidil (0.2 to 24 micromol/L). VEGF mRNA and protein were measured.

**The finding:** Minoxidil induced a dose-dependent increase in VEGF mRNA expression — cells treated with 24 micromol/L expressed *six times* more VEGF mRNA than controls. VEGF protein levels also increased in both cell extracts and conditioned media. Separately, minoxidil is established as an ATP-sensitive potassium channel opener (acting on Kir6/SUR2 channels), causing cell membrane hyperpolarization. The compound is actually a prodrug — it must be converted to minoxidil sulfate by the enzyme sulfotransferase (SULT1A1) in hair follicles to become active. Additional mechanisms include stimulation of prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) synthesis via PGHS-1 activation, which promotes anagen maintenance.

**How much weight to give it:** **Low to Moderate.** The VEGF data is in vitro (cultured cells in a dish), not in vivo on a human scalp. However, this isn't an isolated finding — multiple research groups have confirmed VEGF upregulation, potassium channel opening, and prostaglandin involvement. The mechanistic picture is more complex than "increases blood flow," but most of the molecular evidence comes from cell culture. What matters clinically is that the RCTs prove the *drug* works — the mechanism explains *why*, even if the details are still being refined.

**Citation:** Lachgar S, Charveron M, Gall Y, Bonafe JL. Minoxidil upregulates the expression of vascular endothelial growth factor in human hair dermal papilla cells. *Br J Dermatol.* 1998;138(3):407-411.

## Claim 5: "Minoxidil activates beta-catenin signaling in dermal papilla cells, prolonging anagen"

**The model:** In vitro + in vivo animal. Kwack et al. (2011) treated cultured human dermal papilla cells (DPCs) with minoxidil and measured beta-catenin pathway activation. They also applied minoxidil topically to mice and assessed anagen duration.

**The finding:** Minoxidil stimulated transcriptional activity of the beta-catenin reporter (pTopflash) but not the mutant control (pFopflash), confirming specific Wnt/beta-catenin pathway activation. Nuclear accumulation of beta-catenin was observed in treated DPCs. Minoxidil increased phosphorylation of GSK3-beta, PKA, and PKB — all upstream regulators of beta-catenin stability. It also induced expression of Wnt target genes Axin2 and Lef-1. In the mouse model, topical minoxidil modestly extended anagen duration and delayed catagen entry.

**How much weight to give it:** **Low to Moderate.** The in vitro DPC data is compelling for mechanistic understanding — this is at least testing the right cell type (dermal papilla cells, not cancer cell lines). The mouse in vivo data adds support but mouse hair cycling is fundamentally different from human. Together, this provides a plausible molecular explanation for why minoxidil keeps hairs growing longer before they shed, but the clinical proof comes from the human RCTs, not from this mechanism work.

**Citation:** Kwack MH, Kang BM, Kim MK, et al. Minoxidil activates beta-catenin pathway in human dermal papilla cells: a possible explanation for its anagen prolongation effect. *J Dermatol Sci.* 2011;62(3):154-159.

## Claim 6: "Sulfotransferase activity predicts who will respond to minoxidil — and many people are non-responders"

**The model:** Human observational + enzyme assay. Roberts et al. (2014) developed an enzymatic assay measuring SULT1A1 (sulfotransferase) activity in plucked hair follicles and correlated it with clinical response to topical minoxidil in women with androgenetic alopecia.

**The finding:** Because minoxidil is a prodrug requiring conversion to minoxidil sulfate by SULT1A1 in hair follicles, individuals with low sulfotransferase activity cannot activate the drug effectively. The assay used a cut-off value of 0.4 OD 405 for low follicular sulfotransferase activity. Women scoring above 0.6 AU had meaningfully better results than those just above the cut-off. The assay predicted minoxidil responders with 95% sensitivity and 73% specificity. SULT1A1 activity was significantly lower in the non-responder group. Estimates suggest 30-40% of patients may be non-responders or partial responders due to insufficient follicular sulfotransferase.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate-High.** This is real human data from plucked hair follicles measuring a biologically validated enzyme pathway. It explains a long-standing clinical observation: many patients don't respond to minoxidil. The assay hasn't been widely adopted in clinical practice (most dermatologists don't test sulfotransferase before prescribing), but the biology is sound. This has practical implications — if topical minoxidil isn't working after 6-12 months, you may be a sulfotransferase non-responder, and oral minoxidil (which is activated by hepatic SULT2A1, a different enzyme) may bypass this issue.

**Citation:** Roberts J, Desai N, McCoy J, Bhoyrul S. Sulfotransferase activity in plucked hair follicles predicts response to topical minoxidil in the treatment of female androgenetic alopecia. *Dermatol Ther.* 2014;27(4):252-254. Also: Goren A, et al. Clinical utility and validity of minoxidil response testing in androgenetic alopecia. *Dermatol Ther.* 2015;28(1):13-16.

## Claim 7: "Combining minoxidil with finasteride is more effective than either alone"

**The model:** Human RCT (randomized, comparative). Hu et al. (2015) conducted a 12-month trial in 450 Chinese men with AGA, randomized to oral finasteride 1 mg/day (n=160), topical 5% minoxidil twice daily (n=130), or the combination (n=160). Efficacy was evaluated in 428 men who completed the trial.

**The finding:** At 12 months, improvement rates were: combination therapy 94.1%, finasteride monotherapy 80.5%, and minoxidil monotherapy 59.0%. The combination also produced earlier visible improvement — detectable at 3 months. Adverse reactions were rare: finasteride 1.8%, minoxidil 6.1% (mostly local irritation). The study concluded that finasteride alone was superior to minoxidil alone, while the combination showed the best overall efficacy.

**How much weight to give it:** **High.** This is a large (n=450), well-powered comparative trial with a 12-month endpoint — long enough to see meaningful results. The 94.1% improvement rate with combination therapy is striking. The limitation is that this was not placebo-controlled (all arms received active treatment), and efficacy was assessed by investigator grading rather than precise hair counts. But the clinical message is clear: the two drugs have complementary mechanisms (minoxidil stimulates growth; finasteride blocks the hormonal driver of miniaturization), and combining them outperforms either alone.

**Citation:** Hu R, Xu F, Sheng Y, et al. Combined treatment with oral finasteride and topical minoxidil in male androgenetic alopecia: a randomized and comparative study in Chinese patients. *Dermatol Ther.* 2015;28(5):303-308.

## Claim 8: "Low-dose oral minoxidil is effective for hair loss and has an acceptable safety profile"

**The model:** Human multicenter retrospective safety study. Randolph and Tosti (2021) conducted the largest safety assessment of low-dose oral minoxidil (LDOM) for alopecia, analyzing data from 1,404 patients (943 women, 461 men; mean age 43, range 8-86) treated with LDOM for at least 3 months across multiple centers.

**The finding:** The most frequent adverse effect was hypertrichosis (unwanted body/facial hair growth) at 15.1%, which led to treatment withdrawal in only 0.5%. Systemic adverse effects included lightheadedness (1.7%), fluid retention (1.3%), tachycardia (0.9%), headache (0.4%), periorbital edema (0.3%), and insomnia (0.2%). Only 1.7% of patients overall required drug discontinuation due to adverse effects. No cases of pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade, or serious cardiovascular events were reported at these low doses (0.25-5 mg/day). For context, the FDA's black-box warning for pericardial effusion applies to oral minoxidil at hypertensive doses (10-40 mg/day), where pericardial effusion occurs in about 3% of patients.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate-High.** This is the largest real-world safety dataset for LDOM, and it's reassuring — but it's retrospective, not a randomized trial, so it primarily addresses safety rather than efficacy. The 15.1% hypertrichosis rate is expected and dose-dependent. The very low rates of cardiovascular effects at hair-loss doses (0.25-5 mg) are qualitatively different from the high-dose hypertension literature. Still, any patient starting oral minoxidil should be monitored, especially for fluid retention and heart rate changes, particularly those with pre-existing cardiac conditions.

**Citation:** Randolph M, Tosti A. Safety of low-dose oral minoxidil for hair loss: a multicenter study of 1404 patients. *J Am Acad Dermatol.* 2021;84(6):1758-1764.

## Claim 9: "Oral minoxidil 5 mg is comparable to topical 5% minoxidil for male AGA"

**The model:** Human RCT (randomized clinical trial). Penha et al. (2024) conducted a 24-week RCT published in *JAMA Dermatology* comparing daily oral minoxidil 5 mg with twice-daily topical minoxidil 5% in 90 men with androgenetic alopecia. Hair counts were measured in target areas on both the frontal and vertex scalp.

**The finding:** After 24 weeks, 68 patients completed the trial. There was no statistically significant difference between oral and topical minoxidil in terminal hair count increases on the frontal scalp (+3.1 hairs/cm^2 difference, p=0.27) or total hair count (-2.6 hairs/cm^2, p=0.32). Photographic analysis suggested oral minoxidil may have a modest vertex advantage (24% improvement vs 12%), but the difference was not statistically significant overall. Hypertrichosis was significantly more common with oral minoxidil.

**How much weight to give it:** **Highest.** This is the first head-to-head RCT comparing the two routes, published in a top-tier journal. The key takeaway: oral minoxidil at 5 mg/day does not appear *superior* to topical 5%, but it's comparable — meaning it's a legitimate alternative for patients who can't tolerate or won't adhere to topical application. The trade-off is systemic side effects (especially hypertrichosis) versus local irritation. Interestingly, a separate trial found that oral 2.5 mg may produce similar efficacy to 5 mg with fewer side effects, suggesting dose optimization is still being refined.

**Citation:** Penha MA, Fabbri TR, Weffort F, et al. Oral minoxidil vs topical minoxidil for male androgenetic alopecia: a randomized clinical trial. *JAMA Dermatol.* 2024;160(5):507-513.

## Claim 10: "Microneedling dramatically boosts minoxidil's effectiveness"

**The model:** Human RCT (randomized, evaluator-blinded). Dhurat et al. (2013) enrolled 100 men with mild-to-moderate AGA and randomized them to either weekly microneedling (1.5 mm dermaroller) plus twice-daily 5% minoxidil, or 5% minoxidil alone, for 12 weeks.

**The finding:** The results were striking: at 12 weeks, the microneedling + minoxidil group had a mean hair count increase of 91.4 hairs versus 22.2 hairs for minoxidil alone — a roughly 4-fold difference. On a 7-point visual analogue scale, 40 patients (82%) in the microneedling group rated improvement at +2 to +3, while zero patients in the minoxidil-only group achieved the same response. 82% of microneedling patients reported >50% improvement versus 4.5% of the minoxidil-only group. Microneedling was also noted to rescue some patients who had previously failed minoxidil monotherapy.

**How much weight to give it:** **High.** This is an RCT (evaluator-blinded), and the effect sizes are large — unusually large for a hair loss trial. The main limitations: 12-week duration (short for hair regrowth), single-center, and the 1.5 mm needle depth is aggressive enough to cause bleeding and requires recovery time. It's also a pilot-sized study (n=100 total). Subsequent studies and a meta-analysis have confirmed the synergistic effect, though with more modest effect sizes than this original trial. The proposed mechanism is that microneedling induces wound healing, upregulates growth factors (PDGF, EGF), and may enhance minoxidil penetration through the stratum corneum.

**Citation:** Dhurat R, Sukesh MS, Avhad G, et al. A randomized evaluator blinded study of effect of microneedling in androgenetic alopecia: a pilot study. *Int J Trichology.* 2013;5(1):6-11.

## Claim 11: "Oral minoxidil works for women — including those who fail topical"

**The model:** Human open-label/retrospective. Sinclair (2018) conducted an observational pilot study of 100 women with Sinclair stage 2-5 female pattern hair loss, treated with once-daily oral minoxidil 0.25 mg combined with spironolactone 25 mg for 12 months. In a separate study, Ramos et al. (2019) compared oral minoxidil 1 mg with topical minoxidil 5% in women with FPHL in a 24-week randomized trial.

**The finding:** In the Sinclair 2018 study, mean hair loss severity score improved by 0.85 points at 6 months and 1.3 points at 12 months. Hair shedding scores improved by 2.3 at 6 months and 2.6 at 12 months. Side effects occurred in 8 women (mild). In the Ramos comparison, oral minoxidil 1 mg increased total hair density by 12% versus 7.2% for topical 5% after 24 weeks — a non-significant difference — but oral was superior on the Sinclair hair shedding score. Additionally, a descriptive study of 148 women on LDOM (Rodrigues-Barata 2020) showed 61.5% had clinical improvement, with hypertrichosis in 56% and no cardiovascular events.

**How much weight to give it:** **Moderate-High.** The Sinclair study is observational (no placebo arm), and the Ramos comparison was small and didn't reach statistical significance. But the clinical experience is growing rapidly: oral minoxidil bypasses the sulfotransferase bottleneck (the liver uses SULT2A1, not SULT1A1, to activate the drug), which may explain why some topical non-responders improve on oral. The hypertrichosis rate is notably higher in women, which affects adherence. This is an area where the evidence is accumulating quickly but hasn't yet reached the robustness of the topical RCT data.

**Citation:** Sinclair RD. Female pattern hair loss: a pilot study investigating combination therapy with low-dose oral minoxidil and spironolactone. *Int J Dermatol.* 2018;57(1):104-109. Ramos PM, et al. Minoxidil 1 mg oral versus minoxidil 5% topical solution for the treatment of female-pattern hair loss. *J Am Acad Dermatol.* 2020;82(1):252-253.

## Claim 12: "Minoxidil must be used indefinitely — stopping causes hair loss that may drop below baseline"

**The model:** Human observational. Early discontinuation studies (including Bazzano et al. 1986) evaluated men who had been on topical minoxidil (2-3%) for at least 4 months, then stopped treatment and were followed for changes in hair counts.

**The finding:** Hair counts approximately doubled while on treatment (mean) but most of those recruited hairs were lost upon discontinuation. Four out of ten men had nonvellus hair counts that fell *below* their pre-treatment baseline after stopping. Hair loss typically resumes within 3-6 months of discontinuation. The mechanism: minoxidil prolongs anagen and shortens telogen — when the drug is removed, all the hairs held in an artificially extended anagen phase synchronize into telogen and shed together, often creating a dramatic shedding event that can temporarily exceed the natural rate of loss.

**How much weight to give it:** **High.** This is real human data and consistent with universal clinical experience. Minoxidil is not curative — it's a maintenance treatment. Every dermatologist confirms that stopping leads to loss of gains. The below-baseline finding in some patients is particularly important: it suggests that some of the "maintained" follicles may have been on a trajectory toward miniaturization that was merely paused, not reversed. This has practical implications for treatment planning — patients should understand the commitment before starting.

**Citation:** Bazzano GS, Terezakis N, Galen W. Topical tretinoin for hair growth promotion. *J Am Acad Dermatol.* 1986;15:880-893. Also: Price VH. Treatment of hair loss. *N Engl J Med.* 1999;341:964-973.

## The Evidence That's Missing

Despite decades of use and hundreds of studies, significant gaps remain in the minoxidil evidence base:

- **No large RCTs of low-dose oral minoxidil for hair loss.** The existing evidence for LDOM is almost entirely retrospective or open-label. The Penha 2024 JAMA trial is the first head-to-head RCT, and it was relatively small (n=90). Oral minoxidil prescribing for hair loss is currently off-label, based on emerging data and clinical experience rather than Phase III trials.

- **No validated clinical test for sulfotransferase status is in routine use.** Despite the Roberts 2014 assay showing 95% sensitivity, most dermatologists still prescribe minoxidil empirically and wait 6-12 months to see if it works. A widely available, affordable responder test would save non-responders time and money.

- **No long-term RCT data beyond 48 weeks for topical minoxidil.** The pivotal trials ran for 48 weeks, but patients use this drug for decades. Long-term efficacy and the question of whether response diminishes over years are not well-characterized in controlled settings.

- **No good data on optimal frequency for topical application.** The FDA label says twice daily, but many dermatologists recommend once daily for 5% formulations based on clinical experience. There is no definitive RCT comparing once-daily versus twice-daily 5% minoxidil with long-term follow-up.

- **Limited data on oral minoxidil cardiac safety beyond 12-24 months.** The Randolph 2021 safety study (n=1,404) required at least 3 months of treatment. True long-term cardiovascular data in otherwise healthy hair loss patients on 1-5 mg/day for 5-10+ years doesn't exist.

- **The "dread shed" hasn't been rigorously quantified.** The temporary shedding that occurs 2-8 weeks after starting minoxidil is universally reported but poorly characterized in controlled studies. A 2025 retrospective study suggested that patients who shed more initially may have better long-term outcomes, but prospective data is lacking.

- **No head-to-head trials of microneedling depth and frequency.** The Dhurat study used 1.5 mm weekly. Other studies use different depths and intervals. The optimal microneedling protocol for enhancing minoxidil is not established.

## The Bottom Line

Minoxidil is the most extensively studied hair growth agent in the world. Its efficacy is not in question — multiple large RCTs, across both sexes, confirm that topical 5% minoxidil produces statistically significant and clinically meaningful hair regrowth compared to placebo. The 5% concentration is modestly superior to 2%, the foam formulation eliminates the propylene glycol irritation issue, and combining minoxidil with finasteride produces the best outcomes (94% improvement rate in the Hu 2015 trial).

The oral minoxidil story is newer and less robust, but rapidly maturing. The Randolph 2021 safety study (1,404 patients) and the Penha 2024 JAMA RCT have moved LDOM from "interesting off-label idea" to "legitimate treatment option with real data behind it." Oral appears comparable to topical in efficacy, may work for topical non-responders via a different activation pathway (hepatic SULT2A1 vs follicular SULT1A1), and avoids scalp irritation — but at the cost of higher hypertrichosis rates and the need for cardiovascular monitoring.

The sulfotransferase responder issue deserves more attention than it gets. An estimated 30-40% of patients who try topical minoxidil will have suboptimal responses due to low follicular SULT1A1 activity. If topical isn't working after 6-12 months of consistent use, the answer isn't necessarily "minoxidil doesn't work for you" — it may be "topical doesn't work for you, but oral might."

The biggest limitation of minoxidil isn't efficacy — it's permanence. This is a maintenance drug. Stop it, and the gains reverse within months, sometimes below baseline. For patients weighing whether to start, this is the most important thing to understand: minoxidil is a commitment, not a cure.
