# VDPHL01 (Veradermics) for Hair Loss: What the Science Actually Shows

*A claim-by-claim breakdown of the evidence behind this extended-release oral minoxidil formulation for pattern hair loss, with the experimental model behind each finding and what it means for you.*

---

VDPHL01 is an investigational extended-release oral minoxidil tablet being developed by Veradermics, Inc. (NYSE: MANE) for male and female pattern hair loss. The active ingredient -- minoxidil -- is not new. It has been used for hair growth since the 1980s, both as a topical solution (Rogaine) and increasingly as an off-label oral medication. What is new is the delivery system: a proprietary gel-matrix formulation designed to release minoxidil slowly, maintaining plasma concentrations in what the company calls a "therapeutic window" between the level needed for hair growth and the level at which cardiac side effects begin.

Veradermics completed a $256 million IPO in February 2026, with shares trading on the NYSE under the ticker "MANE." The company has three pivotal trials underway: two in men (~1,000 total patients) and one in women (500+ patients), with topline results from the first trial expected in the first half of 2026.

This is a well-funded, aggressively developed program built on a proven active ingredient. But "proven ingredient in a new formulation" and "proven new product" are different things. Here is every major claim about VDPHL01, matched to the actual evidence behind it.

## How to Read Scientific Evidence: A Quick Hierarchy

Before we get into the claims, you need to understand the evidence ladder. Not all studies are created equal:

| Evidence Level | What It Is | Weight for a Consumer | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **In silico** (computational) | Computer analysis of gene expression databases | Very Low | Predicts what *might* happen based on patterns. No biological system was actually tested. |
| **In vitro** (cell culture) | Cells grown in a dish, treated with a compound | Low | Shows biological activity in isolated cells, but cells in a plastic dish don't behave like cells in your scalp. |
| **In vivo animal** | Living mice, rats, minipigs, etc. | Moderate | Whole-organism data, which matters. But animal skin is not human skin. |
| **Ex vivo** (organ culture) | Real human tissue maintained alive outside the body | Moderate-High | Tissue architecture is intact, but no blood flow, hormones, or immune context. |
| **Human clinical trial (open-label)** | People use the product and results are measured, but no placebo group | High | Real human data. But without a placebo control, you can't separate treatment effect from natural variation. |
| **Human RCT (double-blind, placebo-controlled)** | Gold standard. Patients get treatment or placebo, and neither they nor doctors know which. | Highest | The only design that definitively proves a treatment works. |

Keep this ladder in mind as we go through each claim.

## Claim 1: "Oral minoxidil is a proven hair growth agent with decades of clinical evidence"

**The model:** Multiple randomized controlled trials and systematic reviews evaluating low-dose oral minoxidil (0.625-5 mg) for androgenetic alopecia in both men and women. A 2025 meta-analysis pooled data from 27 studies (10 RCTs and 17 single-arm trials) covering 2,933 patients. A 2024 JAMA Dermatology RCT compared oral minoxidil 5 mg once daily vs. topical minoxidil 5% twice daily over 24 weeks in men.

**The finding:** Oral minoxidil produces clinically meaningful hair growth across multiple studies. In the 24-week RCT, oral minoxidil 5 mg increased total hair count by approximately 19% from baseline, with superior results at the vertex compared to topical minoxidil by photographic assessment (24% improvement). A 2025 meta-analysis found oral minoxidil was comparable in efficacy to topical minoxidil for hair density improvements. The most common adverse effect is hypertrichosis (unwanted body/facial hair) in approximately 15-49% of patients depending on dose, with occasional fluid retention (1.3-10%).

**How much weight to give it: Highest -- but this is not about VDPHL01.** This is the strongest foundational evidence for VDPHL01, because VDPHL01 is fundamentally an oral minoxidil product. The active pharmaceutical ingredient has a robust evidence base -- oral minoxidil genuinely grows hair. Multiple independent research groups across different countries have demonstrated this in controlled trials.

However, this evidence applies to immediate-release oral minoxidil at various doses, not specifically to the extended-release VDPHL01 formulation. The question is not whether oral minoxidil works (it does), but whether the extended-release formulation offers a meaningful advantage in terms of efficacy, safety, or both. That question remains unanswered.

**Citation:** Ramos PM et al., JAMA Dermatol. 2024. PMID: 38598226. | Li Y et al., Front Pharmacol. 2025. doi: 10.3389/fphar.2025.1556705.

## Claim 2: "VDPHL01's extended-release formulation keeps minoxidil plasma levels in a 'therapeutic window' between hair growth and cardiac thresholds"

**The model:** Pharmacokinetic studies in humans as part of VDPHL01 clinical development. According to company disclosures (SEC S-1 filing and press releases), the extended-release gel-matrix formulation was designed to maintain sustained plasma minoxidil concentrations above ~1-2 ng/mL (associated with hair growth) while remaining below ~20 ng/mL (the level associated with cardiac activity onset). The company reports approximately a 10-fold separation between these thresholds.

**The finding:** Veradermics reports that VDPHL01's extended-release profile maintains minoxidil in circulation for 7.5-12 hours with once-daily dosing and 15-24 hours with twice-daily dosing. The formulation purportedly avoids the rapid plasma spikes seen with immediate-release oral minoxidil, which can briefly exceed cardiac thresholds even at low doses. No cardiac adverse events were reported in the Phase 2 trial of 21 male patients receiving 8.5 mg twice daily for 4 months.

**How much weight to give it: Moderate for the concept, low for the specific claims.** The pharmacokinetic rationale is the core innovation claimed by Veradermics. The concept makes biological sense: immediate-release oral minoxidil produces rapid Cmax spikes that can cause transient cardiac effects (pericardial effusion, reflex tachycardia, fluid retention), while the actual hair-growth-promoting plasma level may be much lower. An extended-release formulation that flattens the PK curve could theoretically deliver more total drug exposure in the hair-growth range while staying below the cardiac risk threshold.

However, the specific plasma concentration thresholds cited by the company (1-2 ng/mL for hair growth, 20 ng/mL for cardiac activity) are not established by independent peer-reviewed research as precise cut-points. Minoxidil's cardiac effects are dose-dependent and patient-dependent, not governed by a single bright-line threshold. The pharmacokinetic data itself has not been published in a peer-reviewed journal -- it comes from the company's SEC filing and press releases. Additionally, whether sustained lower-level exposure produces better hair growth than brief higher-level exposure is an assumption that the pivotal trials are designed to test but have not yet proven.

**Citation:** Veradermics S-1 Registration Statement, SEC, 2026. | Veradermics press release, October 2025 (BusinessWire).

## Claim 3: "Preliminary Phase 2 data shows +47.3 hairs/cm2 increase from baseline in men at 4 months"

**The model:** Open-label, multi-center Phase 2 trial (NCT06527365). 21 male participants with mild-to-moderate androgenetic alopecia received VDPHL01 8.5 mg twice daily. Non-vellus target area hair count was measured from baseline at 2-month and 4-month time points. This trial had no placebo arm.

**The finding:** At 2 months, the 21 male participants achieved a mean increase of +37.5 hairs/cm2 from baseline. At 4 months, the mean increase reached +47.3 hairs/cm2 from baseline. Additionally, 90.5% of patients reported "improved" or "much improved" hair coverage at 4 months (55% at 2 months). No serious adverse events were reported, including no cardiac adverse events.

**How much weight to give it: Low to Moderate.** The +47.3 hairs/cm2 at 4 months is a large absolute number -- larger than what topical minoxidil 5% typically achieves in comparable timeframes (typically +15-25 hairs/cm2 including the placebo effect). But this comparison is fundamentally misleading without a placebo arm.

Open-label trials routinely produce inflated results due to multiple biases: placebo effect, regression to the mean, measurement bias (knowing everyone is on active drug can unconsciously influence hair counting), and selection of patients who are motivated and adherent. In the TDM-105795 Phase 2a trial (a different investigational hair loss drug), the placebo group gained +14 hairs/cm2 -- accounting for 57% of the high-dose group's total change. A similar placebo effect in the VDPHL01 data would substantially reduce the net treatment effect.

The 90.5% patient-reported improvement rate is even more susceptible to open-label bias -- patients who know they are taking a new hair loss drug and are being closely monitored are highly motivated to perceive improvement.

With only 21 patients, no placebo, no published standard deviations, no peer review, and data reported in press releases timed to a $150M Series C financing round, this data should be viewed as a preliminary signal, not evidence of efficacy. The pivotal Phase 2/3 and Phase 3 RCTs with placebo controls will provide the real answer.

**Citation:** Veradermics press release, October 16, 2025 (BusinessWire). | ClinicalTrials.gov NCT06527365.

## Claim 4: "Phase 2/3 and Phase 3 pivotal RCTs are underway with ~1,000 male patients enrolled"

**The model:** Two pivotal, multi-center, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials in male patients with mild-to-moderate pattern hair loss:

1. **'302' Phase 2/3 trial (NCT06724614):** 519 male patients, evaluating VDPHL01 8.5 mg once daily vs. twice daily vs. placebo over 52 weeks. Co-primary endpoints: change in non-vellus hair count and patient-reported hair coverage benefit at 24 weeks. Enrollment completed December 2025.

2. **'304' Phase 3 confirmatory trial:** 536 male patients with the same design and endpoints. Enrollment completed February 2026.

An additional Phase 2/3 trial in 500+ female patients (NCT07146022) evaluating VDPHL01 4.5 mg is actively recruiting.

**The finding:** No efficacy or safety results have been reported from these placebo-controlled trials. Topline data from the '302' trial is anticipated in H1 2026; '304' topline data is expected in H2 2026.

**How much weight to give it: Moderate for the study design quality, but no results exist yet.** The pivotal trial program is well-designed: randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, with adequate enrollment (~500+ per trial), 52-week treatment duration, and co-primary endpoints including both objective hair counts and patient-reported outcomes.

The total enrollment of approximately 1,000 male patients is comparable to (though smaller than) the pivotal finasteride trials (~1,500 men). The 52-week duration is appropriate for a hair loss trial. The inclusion of a patient-reported outcome as a co-primary endpoint is a notable strength -- it means the company must demonstrate that patients perceive meaningful benefit, not just that microscopic hair counts increase.

However, these trials have not yet reported results. The design is sound, but the outcome is unknown. It is entirely possible that VDPHL01's extended-release formulation does not outperform placebo, or that it does but with clinically insignificant margins, or that safety signals emerge in the larger population. Until topline data is available, this claim reflects potential, not evidence.

**Citation:** ClinicalTrials.gov NCT06724614, NCT07146022. | Veradermics press releases, December 2025 and February 2026 (BusinessWire).

## Claim 5: "VDPHL01 may be safer than immediate-release oral minoxidil by avoiding cardiac-threshold plasma spikes"

**The model:** Safety assessment from the Phase 2 open-label trial (NCT06527365) of 21 male patients receiving VDPHL01 8.5 mg twice daily for 4 months, plus pharmacokinetic modeling data described in the SEC S-1 filing. For context, off-label immediate-release oral minoxidil for hair loss is typically prescribed at 0.625-5 mg once daily. FDA-approved Loniten (for severe hypertension) uses 10-40 mg/day.

**The finding:** No serious adverse events were reported in the 21 male Phase 2 participants, including no cardiac adverse events. The company reports that the extended-release formulation avoids plasma concentration spikes above the cardiac activity threshold despite delivering a total daily dose of 17 mg (8.5 mg BID).

**How much weight to give it: Low to Moderate.** The absence of cardiac events in 21 patients is a necessary but insufficient safety signal. Pericardial effusion, the most concerning cardiac risk with systemic minoxidil, occurs in approximately 3% of patients at higher doses -- meaning you would expect to see zero events in a group of 21 people purely by chance even if the risk were identical to immediate-release.

The total daily dose is striking: 17 mg/day (8.5 mg BID) is 3-4x higher than the 2.5-5 mg/day typically prescribed off-label. If the extended-release formulation truly flattens the PK curve enough to make this safe, that would be a meaningful pharmacological advance. But this claim requires validation in the pivotal trials with hundreds of patients and 12 months of follow-up.

A 1,404-patient multicenter safety study by Gupta et al. (2022) found that immediate-release low-dose oral minoxidil (typically 1.25-5 mg/day) was generally well-tolerated, with hypertrichosis as the main side effect. The question for VDPHL01 is whether its much higher dose delivered via extended release is truly safer or equivalent -- or whether longer exposure reveals adverse effects not seen in short, small, open-label data.

**Citation:** Gupta AK et al., J Am Acad Dermatol. 2022. PMID: 34757163. | Veradermics S-1 Filing, SEC, 2026.

## Claim 6: "Extended-release oral delivery solves the compliance and tolerability problems of topical minoxidil"

**The model:** Multiple published studies documenting topical minoxidil adherence challenges, plus RCTs comparing oral vs. topical minoxidil. Topical minoxidil requires twice-daily application, can cause scalp irritation (especially propylene glycol-based formulations), leaves residue, and has poor long-term compliance.

**The finding:** Published RCTs show oral minoxidil achieves comparable hair growth to topical minoxidil while eliminating scalp irritation, application residue, and the twice-daily application burden. However, oral administration introduces systemic side effects not seen with topical use: hypertrichosis (unwanted facial/body hair growth) in 15-49% of patients depending on dose, and fluid retention in 1.3-10%.

**How much weight to give it: Moderate for the oral vs. topical advantage, but this is not VDPHL01-specific.** This is a legitimate advantage of oral over topical delivery and one of the strongest arguments for VDPHL01's commercial proposition. Topical minoxidil has a well-documented compliance problem -- studies consistently show 50%+ discontinuation rates within the first year.

An oral pill eliminates these barriers. But it introduces different ones. At the 8.5 mg BID dose VDPHL01 is testing in men, the hypertrichosis rate could be significant. Whether the extended-release formulation reduces this side effect compared to immediate-release at equivalent total daily doses is unknown.

Importantly, the compliance argument applies to oral minoxidil generally, not specifically to VDPHL01's extended-release formulation. Off-label immediate-release oral minoxidil at 2.5-5 mg/day already solves the topical compliance problem at a fraction of what a branded product will cost.

**Citation:** Ramos PM et al., JAMA Dermatol. 2024. PMID: 38598226. | Sinclair RD et al., J Am Acad Dermatol. 2019. PMID: 31770601.

## Claim 7: "VDPHL01 would be the first FDA-approved oral treatment specifically for hair regrowth in both men and women"

**The model:** Regulatory landscape analysis. Currently, no oral medication is FDA-approved specifically for hair regrowth. Finasteride (Propecia) is approved for male AGA. Oral minoxidil (Loniten) is approved for severe hypertension. No oral treatment is FDA-approved for female pattern hair loss.

**The finding:** If approved, VDPHL01 would be the first FDA-approved extended-release oral minoxidil and the first non-hormonal oral treatment approved specifically for hair regrowth in both sexes. The female pattern hair loss indication is particularly notable. Veradermics is pursuing a 505(b)(2) regulatory pathway, which relies on the FDA's prior findings of safety and efficacy for the reference listed drug (immediate-release minoxidil) combined with new data for the novel formulation.

**How much weight to give it: Low as a scientific claim, meaningful as a regulatory milestone.** Being "first" is a commercial and regulatory milestone, not a scientific one. The relevant question for patients is not whether VDPHL01 is the first FDA-approved oral minoxidil for hair loss, but whether it offers clinically meaningful advantages over the off-label oral minoxidil that thousands of dermatologists already prescribe at a fraction of the cost.

That said, FDA approval carries real value: it provides standardized dosing guidelines, mandates controlled manufacturing, generates large-scale safety data, and opens the door for insurance coverage and broader physician adoption. Many dermatologists are reluctant to prescribe off-label oral minoxidil due to liability concerns, so an FDA-approved formulation could meaningfully expand patient access. The female PHL indication is especially important given the lack of approved options for women.

**Citation:** Veradermics S-1 Registration Statement, SEC, 2026. | Villani A et al., Clin Drug Investig. 2024.

## The Evidence That's Missing

After matching every claim to its model, here is what the evidence picture looks like:

**What exists:**
- Decades of RCT evidence proving oral minoxidil grows hair (multiple independent research groups)
- Pharmacokinetic modeling claiming a 10x safety window between hair-growth and cardiac thresholds (company-reported, not peer-reviewed)
- One open-label Phase 2 study of 21 men showing +47.3 hairs/cm2 at 4 months (no placebo, not peer-reviewed)
- Three pivotal Phase 2/3 and Phase 3 RCTs enrolled with ~1,500 total patients (results pending)

**What does not exist:**
- **Any placebo-controlled efficacy data for VDPHL01.** All available efficacy data comes from an uncontrolled, open-label Phase 2 study of 21 men. The true treatment effect over placebo is unknown.
- **Head-to-head comparison with immediate-release oral minoxidil.** This is the most important missing study. If VDPHL01 is fundamentally just extended-release minoxidil, the relevant comparison is not to placebo alone but to the cheap, widely available immediate-release form already prescribed off-label.
- **Long-term safety data at the VDPHL01 dose (8.5 mg BID = 17 mg/day).** This is 3-4x higher than typical off-label doses. Cardiovascular safety, hypertrichosis rates, and fluid retention at this dose over 12+ months are unknown.
- **Published pharmacokinetic data.** The PK claims come from company disclosures, not peer-reviewed publications. No independent research group has characterized the formulation.
- **Female-specific data.** The Phase 2 interim results are from male patients only. The female Phase 2/3 trial is still recruiting.
- **Any peer-reviewed publication of VDPHL01-specific data.** As of March 2026, all VDPHL01 evidence comes from company press releases, the SEC S-1 filing, and ClinicalTrials.gov registry entries.

## A Note on the Patent Composition

It is worth noting that Veradermics' patent application (WO2024091572A1) describes a formulation containing not only minoxidil but also cetirizine, setipiprant, valproic acid, and medrogestone. The company's public communications and SEC filings describe VDPHL01 exclusively as an extended-release oral minoxidil product, without mentioning these additional compounds. Whether the clinical formulation contains only minoxidil or includes some or all of these additional agents is not publicly clarified. This discrepancy between patent claims and public messaging has been noted by independent analysts, including CognitaRx. If VDPHL01 contains additional active ingredients beyond minoxidil, this raises separate efficacy and safety questions that are not addressed by the existing oral minoxidil evidence base.

## The Bottom Line

VDPHL01 is an extended-release oral formulation of minoxidil, a drug whose hair growth efficacy is already well-proven through decades of clinical use. The active ingredient is not new -- what is new is the delivery system. Veradermics' proprietary gel-matrix technology is designed to flatten the pharmacokinetic curve, keeping minoxidil plasma levels in a sustained "therapeutic window" that promotes hair growth while theoretically avoiding the cardiac side effects associated with plasma spikes from immediate-release formulations.

The preliminary Phase 2 data from 21 men is attention-grabbing: +47.3 hairs/cm2 from baseline at 4 months, with no cardiac adverse events despite a total daily dose of 17 mg. But this is open-label, uncontrolled data from a tiny cohort, reported in press releases timed to fundraising events. Without a placebo arm, the true treatment effect is unknown. Without peer review, the data cannot be independently scrutinized.

The real test is coming: three pivotal randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials enrolling approximately 1,500+ patients across men and women, with topline results expected in 2026. These trials are well-designed, with 52-week treatment duration, co-primary endpoints including patient-reported outcomes, and adequate enrollment. If positive, VDPHL01 could become the first FDA-approved oral treatment for hair regrowth in both sexes -- a genuinely significant regulatory milestone, especially for women with pattern hair loss who have no approved oral options.

The elephant in the room is that low-dose oral minoxidil is already widely prescribed off-label and costs a few dollars per month. VDPHL01 must demonstrate that its extended-release formulation is meaningfully better -- enough to justify FDA approval, a premium price, and the commercial proposition of a publicly traded company that raised over $400 million before reporting a single placebo-controlled data point for its product. The pivotal trial data will determine whether this is a genuine pharmacological advance or a well-financed reformulation of a generic drug.

**The strongest honest claim you can make about VDPHL01:** It is an extended-release formulation of a proven hair growth drug, backed by promising but uncontrolled preliminary data and a well-designed pivotal trial program. The active ingredient works. The formulation innovation is plausible. The company is well-funded. But the specific product has not yet been proven in a placebo-controlled trial, and whether it offers meaningful advantages over generic oral minoxidil remains an open question. Patients should not delay proven treatments while waiting for VDPHL01 data.
